\section{Methods}

\subsection{Patient Cohort and MRI Acquisition}

Retrospective multi-b DWI data were obtained from the cohort previously described by \citet{langkilde2018evaluation}, acquired under an institutional review board--approved protocol.
For the 56 included patients, age ranged from 43 to 77 years (mean 62 years) and the mean serum prostate-specific antigen (PSA) level was 8.8~ng/mL (range, 0.37--46~ng/mL).
The cohort spanned the diagnostic spectrum encountered at biopsy, including confirmed adenocarcinoma as well as benign and high-grade prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia (HGPIN) findings.

All patients underwent a standard multiparametric MR examination with an additional diffusion-weighted sequence on a GE 3~T Discovery MR750 scanner (GE Medical Systems, Milwaukee, WI).
An endorectal coil (Medrad, Pittsburgh, PA) was used in combination with pelvic phased-array coils for signal reception, providing high signal-to-noise ratio at the cost of a non-routine setup.
The added DWI sequence used a matrix size of $64 \times 64$, field of view $280 \times 280\;\text{mm}^2$, slice thickness 5~mm without gap, TR~4000~ms, and TE~100~ms.
The relatively long echo time was required to accommodate the high diffusion weighting with gradient times $\delta = 37$~ms and $\Delta = 45$~ms.
Fifteen b-values were acquired in 250~s/mm$^2$ increments from $b = 0$ to $3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ along each of three orthogonal diffusion-encoding directions, combined into a trace decay (geometric mean across directions) per b-value; total scan time for the extended-range acquisition was 3~min.

On the multiparametric images an expert radiologist delineated, for each patient, a focal suspicious lesion (tumor) where present together with reference normal tissue in the peripheral and transition zones. For each of these ROIs the trace signal was averaged over the segmented voxels at every b-value and paired with the patient's biopsy Gleason score.
A total of 149 ROIs were analyzed---40 tumor and 109 normal---spanning the peripheral zone (PZ; $n = 81$: 27 tumor, 54 normal) and transition zone (TZ; $n = 68$: 13 tumor, 55 normal).
Of the 40 tumor ROIs, 29 were biopsy-confirmed adenocarcinoma with a Gleason Grade Group (GGG~1, $n = 8$; GGG~2, $n = 12$; GGG~3, $n = 5$; GGG~4, $n = 2$; GGG~5, $n = 2$); the remainder were benign (7), HGPIN (1), or unbiopsied (3), so detection distinguishes radiologically suspicious lesions from normal tissue rather than confirmed cancer alone.
These 29 graded cancers (20 low-grade, GGG~1--2; 9 high-grade, GGG~3--5; PZ 21, TZ 8) form the grading cohort.

\subsection{Spectral Estimation}

For each ROI, the trace signal decay was decomposed into eight diffusivity components ($D \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 20.0\}\;\umsmm$) using both MAP and fully Bayesian estimation as described in Theory section.

MAP spectra were computed via Eq.~\ref{eq:map} and normalized to sum to one (fractional representation); the constrained least-squares solve used SciPy \citep{virtanen2020scipy} and was instantaneous ($< 1\;\text{s}$ for all 149 ROIs).
The ridge weight $\lambda$ governs a bias--variance trade-off that materially shapes the recovered spectrum: too small a value lets the ill-conditioned fit chase noise, whereas too large a value over-smooths, pulling mass out of the well-determined restricted and free-water bins and spreading it across the correlated intermediate bins. We set $\lambda = 10^{-3}$ by maximizing recovery of simulated ground-truth spectra (below).

The Bayesian posterior (Eqs.~\ref{eq:prior_R}--\ref{eq:likelihood}) was sampled independently for each of the 149 ROIs using NUTS via PyMC \citep{abrilpla2023pymc} under the half-normal spectrum prior (scale $\sigma_R = \sqrt{10} \approx 3.16$, precision $1/\sigma_R^2 = 0.1$) and a weakly informative half-Cauchy noise prior (scale $0.01$), with 2000 post-tuning draws, 200 tuning steps, 4 independent chains, and target acceptance rate 0.95 (above the PyMC default of 0.8, to limit divergences on the correlated posterior); sampling required on the order of 15--40~s per ROI on an 8-core Apple M1~Pro (roughly 1~h for all 149, run sequentially), versus the sub-second MAP solve.
The noise level $\sigma$ was inferred jointly with the spectrum for each ROI rather than fixed from the acquisition; the resulting per-ROI SNR ($1/\hat{\sigma}$) had cohort median 303 (interquartile range 176--478).
Convergence was assessed using the rank-normalized split-$\hat{R}$ statistic \citep{vehtari2021rank}, computed with ArviZ \citep{kumar2019arviz}; all 149 ROIs converged ($\hat{R} < 1.05$).
Posterior means of the spectral fractions (normalized to sum to one) were used as NUTS point estimates; posterior standard deviations quantify per-component uncertainty, summarized per component by the cohort-averaged coefficient of variation (CV $=$ posterior std / posterior mean).

Both estimators were validated on six simulated ground-truth spectra of varying shape (smooth realistic, uniform, concentrated, and bimodal), each reconstructed at SNR 50, 300, and 1000 over 100 noise realizations (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}).
On the smooth, physiologically realistic spectra, MAP at $\lambda = 10^{-3}$ and NUTS recovered the ground truth equivalently and tightened with increasing SNR; the concentrated $\delta$ spike was only partially recovered and smeared into its correlated neighboring bins---the identifiability limit of this b-value grid (Theory), not a sampler artifact---with NUTS recovering more of the peak than MAP.
The jointly inferred NUTS noise level tracked the truth ($\hat{\sigma}/\sigma_{\mathrm{true}} \approx 1$) across all shapes, whereas the post-hoc MAP residual under- or over-estimated $\sigma$ depending on spectrum shape, confirming that the noise level is better quantified by the joint Bayesian inference than by a residual plug-in.

\subsection{ADC Computation and Classification}

Apparent diffusion coefficients were computed by log-linear least-squares fitting of $\log S(b) = \log S_0 - b \cdot \text{ADC}$ over the five b-values $\leq 1000\;\text{s/mm}^2$ ($b = 0, 250, 500, 750, 1000\;\text{s/mm}^2$)---a monoexponential fit with maximum b-value $1000\;\text{s/mm}^2$, following PI-RADS~v2.1 \citep{turkbey2019prostate}; the b-value range materially affects the absolute ADC value \citep{maier2022choice}.

Tumor detection was evaluated by zone with $L_2$-regularized logistic regression under leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV): tumor versus normal in the peripheral zone ($n = 81$) and the transition zone ($n = 68$), fit separately because normal peripheral- and transition-zone tissue differ systematically in baseline diffusion whereas tumor tissue is comparatively similar across zones, so a pooled boundary would conflate zonal and pathological variation.
Five feature representations were scored through one common pipeline---ADC alone, each individual spectral fraction alone, the restricted and free-water fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ jointly, the five intermediate fractions ($D = 0.5$--$2.0\;\umsmm$; with the restricted, free-water, and $D = 20\;\umsmm$ vascular pseudo-diffusion fractions removed), and all eight fractions (MAP or NUTS posterior means)---so that single- and multi-feature models are directly comparable.
Within each fold, features were standardized to zero mean and unit variance using statistics from the training ROIs only. Because the eight fractions differ several-fold in scale and cross-ROI variance, this keeps the $L_2$ penalty from shrinking coefficients by feature scale rather than discriminative value and makes the per-bin coefficients comparable as the change in tumor log-odds per standard deviation of each fraction, a scale-free measure of importance.
The classifier ($C = 1.0$) was then fit on the training ROIs---the $n - 1$ remaining ROIs in the zone (80 in the peripheral zone, 67 in the transition zone)---and applied to the held-out ROI, with fitting confined to the training fold to prevent leakage; the conventional in-sample ADC raw-rank AUC is additionally reported as a reference baseline (Table~\ref{tab:auc}).
Performance was the area under the receiver-operating-characteristic curve (AUC), with $95\%$ confidence intervals from patient-level percentile bootstrap (2000 resamples) \citep{efron1993bootstrap}; sensitivity analyses at $C = 0.1$ and $C = 10$ confirmed robustness to regularization strength.
