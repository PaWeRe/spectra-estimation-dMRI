\section{Methods}

\subsection{Patient Cohort and MRI Acquisition}

Retrospective multi-b DWI data were obtained from the cohort previously described by \citet{langkilde2018evaluation}.
For the 56 included patients, age ranged from 43 to 77 years (mean 62 years) and the mean serum prostate-specific antigen (PSA) level was 8.8~ng/mL (range, 0.37--46~ng/mL).
The cohort included patients with biopsy-confirmed prostate cancer, 7 patients with benign biopsies, and 1 patient with high-grade prostatic intraepithelial neoplasia (HGPIN, a known tumor precursor).
The benign and HGPIN cases contribute only normal-tissue ROIs to the analysis and therefore serve as a specificity test against false-positive labeling of non-malignant tissue.

All patients underwent a standard multiparametric MR examination with an additional diffusion-weighted sequence on a GE 3~T Discovery MR750 scanner (GE Medical Systems, Milwaukee, WI).
An endorectal coil (Medrad, Pittsburgh, PA) was used in combination with pelvic phased-array coils for signal reception, providing high signal-to-noise ratio at the cost of a non-routine setup.
The added DWI sequence used a matrix size of $64 \times 64$, field of view $280 \times 280\;\text{mm}^2$, slice thickness 5~mm without gap, TR~4000~ms, and TE~100~ms.
The relatively long echo time was required to accommodate the high diffusion weighting with gradient times $\delta = 37$~ms and $\Delta = 45$~ms.
Fifteen b-values were acquired in 250~s/mm$^2$ increments from $b = 0$ to $3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ along each of three orthogonal diffusion encoding directions; total scan time for the extended-range acquisition was 3~min.
For the ROI-level analysis, the per-direction signal intensities were combined into a trace decay (geometric mean of the three orthogonal directions per b-value).

A total of 149 regions of interest (ROIs) were annotated: 40 tumor and 109 normal tissue, spanning peripheral zone (PZ; 81 ROIs: 27 tumor, 54 normal) and transition zone (TZ; 68 ROIs: 13 tumor, 55 normal).
Gleason Grade Group (GGG) labels from biopsy were available for 29 tumor ROIs (20 low-grade GGG~1--2, 9 high-grade GGG~3--5).

\subsection{Spectral Estimation}

For each ROI, the trace signal decay was decomposed into eight diffusivity components ($D \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 20.0\}\;\umsmm$) using both MAP and fully Bayesian estimation as described in Theory.

MAP spectra were computed via Eq.~\ref{eq:map} with ridge strength $\lambda = 10^{-3}$, tuned to optimize spectral recovery on simulated data, then normalized to sum to one (fractional representation); computation was instantaneous ($< 1\;\text{s}$ for all 149 ROIs).
The Bayesian posterior (Eqs.~\ref{eq:prior_R}--\ref{eq:likelihood}) was sampled independently for each of the 149 ROIs using NUTS via PyMC, with 2000 post-tuning draws, 200 tuning steps, 4 independent chains, and target acceptance rate 0.95.
Convergence was assessed using the split-$\hat{R}$ statistic \citep{vehtari2021rank}; all 149 ROIs converged ($\hat{R} < 1.05$).
Posterior means of the spectral fractions (normalized to sum to one) were used as NUTS point estimates; posterior standard deviations quantify per-component uncertainty.
The posterior coefficient of variation (CV $=$ posterior std / posterior mean) reported in the Results is computed per ROI and per component, then averaged across the patient cohort to summarize the typical identifiability of each component for this acquisition.

\subsection{ADC Computation}

Apparent diffusion coefficients were computed by monoexponential least-squares fitting of $\log S(b) = \log S_0 - b \cdot \text{ADC}$ to the raw signal at b-values $\leq 1000\;\text{s/mm}^2$ (five measurements per ROI: $b = 0, 250, 500, 750, 1000\;\text{s/mm}^2$).
This cutoff was chosen to follow PI-RADS~v2.1 recommendations, which specify a maximum b-value of 800--1000~s/mm$^2$ for ADC map calculation \citep{turkbey2019prostate, weinreb2016pirads}; the choice of b-value range materially affects the absolute ADC value \citep{maier2022choice}.

\subsection{Classification}

Tumor detection and Gleason grade classification were evaluated using $L_2$-regularized logistic regression with leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV).
Three classification tasks were defined: (1)~tumor versus normal in PZ ($n = 81$), (2)~tumor versus normal in TZ ($n = 68$), and (3)~high-grade (GGG~$\geq 3$) versus low-grade (GGG~1--2) among tumor ROIs with available GGG labels ($n = 29$).
Detection classifiers were trained separately by zone because the diffusion contrast between zones is largely a property of \emph{normal} tissue---normal peripheral- and transition-zone tissue differ systematically in baseline diffusion, whereas tumor tissue is comparatively similar across zones---so a single pooled tumor-versus-normal boundary would conflate zonal and pathological variation.

For each task, the following feature representations were compared through an identical leave-one-out cross-validation (LOOCV) pipeline: (a)~ADC alone; (b)~each individual spectral fraction alone; (c)~the two outer fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ jointly; and (d)~all eight fractions, using either MAP or NUTS posterior-mean estimates.
Within each fold, features were standardized and an $L_2$-regularized logistic-regression classifier ($C = 1.0$, inverse regularization strength) was fit on the training ROIs and applied to the held-out ROI, with standardization and fitting confined to the training fold to prevent data leakage.
Scoring single- and multi-feature models through the same held-out pipeline makes them directly comparable: a single monotonic feature incurs negligible overfitting, whereas a multivariate model pays a finite-sample penalty, so this avoids the optimistic bias that arises from scoring single features in-sample.
For reference we additionally report the conventional in-sample ADC raw-rank AUC (Table~\ref{tab:auc}).
Performance was measured by the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC).
Ninety-five percent confidence intervals for all AUCs and for the ROI-level ADC--discriminant correlations were obtained by patient-level percentile bootstrap (2000 resamples) \citep{efron1993bootstrap}; paired comparisons between two AUCs computed on the same ROIs used DeLong's test \citep{delong1988comparing}.
Sensitivity analyses at $C = 0.1$ and $C = 10.0$ confirmed that results were robust to regularization strength.

\subsection{ADC Sensitivity Analysis}

The ADC sensitivity vector (Eq.~\ref{eq:sensitivity}) was computed numerically at the average tumor and average normal spectra for each zone, using finite differences with step size $\epsilon = 10^{-4}$.
Logistic regression coefficients were obtained by fitting the full model (all eight features) to the entire dataset.
The Pearson and Spearman rank correlations between the sensitivity vector and the LR coefficient vector (both eight-dimensional) were computed to quantify alignment.
Bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals for ROI-level correlations between ADC and the spectral discriminant were obtained by resampling ROI pairs with replacement (10,000 resamples).

\subsection{Pixel-wise and Direction-wise Spectral Estimation}

To demonstrate the framework at the voxel level, pixel-wise spectral decomposition was performed on a single axial slice from one patient acquired outside the patient cohort described above, with the prostate manually segmented prior to analysis.
For each voxel, the multi-b signal decay $\svec = [S(b_1), \ldots, S(b_{15})]^T$ was normalized by the $b = 0$ signal and decomposed using both MAP (Eq.~\ref{eq:map}) and NUTS (Eqs.~\ref{eq:prior_R}--\ref{eq:likelihood}) with the same parameters as the ROI-level analysis.
A spectral discriminant score was computed at each voxel by applying the PZ logistic regression coefficients to the pixel-level normalized spectra; for the Bayesian estimates, uncertainty in the discriminant was propagated by drawing 500 Monte Carlo samples from the per-voxel posterior and passing each through the discriminant function.

To verify the trace-averaging assumption used at the ROI level, a separate acquisition that retained the per-encoding-direction signal---13 ROIs across 7 patients, with the same 15 b-values along three orthogonal directions---was decomposed independently for each direction with the MAP estimator and compared to the trace-averaged spectrum (Figure~\ref{fig:directions}).

Code, configuration files, and the de-identified per-ROI signal decays used in this study are openly available at \url{https://github.com/PaWeRe/spectra-estimation-dMRI}.
The repository also packages the inference methods (closed-form ridge MAP and PyMC NUTS) so that the full pipeline can be re-run end-to-end on either the released data or new datasets.
