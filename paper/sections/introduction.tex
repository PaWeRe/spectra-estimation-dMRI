\section{Introduction}

Diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (DWI) is a cornerstone of prostate cancer diagnosis, incorporated into the Prostate Imaging Reporting and Data System (PI-RADS) as a primary determinant of clinical significance \citep{turkbey2019prostate, weinreb2016pirads}.
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC), derived from monoexponential fitting of the DWI signal at two or more b-values, has become the most widely used quantitative biomarker for prostate cancer detection and grading \citep{manetta2019correlation, yan2024value}.
Lower ADC values indicate restricted water diffusion associated with increased cellular density in malignant tissue, and ADC has demonstrated strong discriminative performance for both tumor detection and Gleason grade differentiation \citep{donati2014prostate, rosenkrantz2015whole}.

However, the monoexponential ADC model collapses the multi-b signal decay into a single scalar, discarding information about the heterogeneous tissue microstructure that generates the observed signal.
Extended b-value protocols spanning $b = 0$ to $3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ reveal non-monoexponential signal behavior arising from multiple diffusion compartments---including restricted intracellular water, hindered extracellular diffusion, glandular luminal water, and vascular pseudo-diffusion \citep{lebihan1988separation, langkilde2018evaluation}.
Multi-compartment models, including intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) \citep{lebihan1988separation} and discrete multi-exponential fitting \citep{conlin2021improved}, have been proposed to recover this microstructural detail.
\citet{langkilde2018evaluation} applied multi-component fitting to extended-range b-value prostate DWI and demonstrated that compartment-specific fractions provided complementary information to ADC for tissue characterization.

A fundamental challenge in multi-compartment diffusivity estimation is the ill-posedness of the inverse Laplace transform: recovering a spectrum of diffusivities from a finite, noisy set of b-value measurements is inherently underdetermined \citep{prange2009quantifying}.
Point estimation methods---including non-negative least squares and regularized regression---yield a single spectrum without quantifying how reliably each component is determined by the data.
Bayesian approaches address this limitation by sampling from the full posterior distribution, providing per-component uncertainty estimates that distinguish well-constrained spectral features from those that are effectively unconstrained by the measurements \citep{prange2009quantifying, wells2022estimation}.

In this work, we present a Bayesian spectral decomposition framework for multi-b prostate DWI and apply it to a cohort of 56 patients (149 regions of interest).
We estimate diffusivity spectra using both a maximum \emph{a~posteriori} (MAP) point estimate and full Bayesian inference via the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts}, and evaluate both approaches for tumor detection and Gleason grade classification.
Our central finding is that ADC is a near-optimal spectral discriminant: across patients, ADC scores anti-correlate with the learned spectral tumor classifier at $r \approx -0.98$, and the ADC sensitivity vector closely aligns with the optimal classification direction.
This result is consistent across anatomical zones and regularization settings, and provides a formal explanation of why ADC---despite collapsing spectral information---performs nearly as well as multi-component classifiers for tumor detection.
The Bayesian analysis additionally reveals that only two of the eight spectral components are well identified by the data (restricted diffusion at $D = 0.25\;\text{\textmu m}^2/\text{ms}$ and free water at $D = 3.0\;\text{\textmu m}^2/\text{ms}$), while intermediate components remain poorly constrained---a finding that has direct implications for the design of future multi-compartment DWI protocols.
We demonstrate the framework at the pixel level on a single patient, producing spectral fraction maps, tumor probability maps, and per-voxel uncertainty estimates.
