\section{Introduction}

Diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (DWI) is a cornerstone of prostate cancer diagnosis, incorporated into the Prostate Imaging Reporting and Data System (PI-RADS) as a primary determinant of clinical significance \citep{turkbey2019prostate, weinreb2016pirads}.
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC), derived from monoexponential fitting of the DWI signal, is the most widely used quantitative biomarker for prostate cancer, with strong discriminative performance for both tumor detection and Gleason grade differentiation \citep{manetta2019correlation, yan2024value, donati2014prostate, rosenkrantz2015whole}.
Lower ADC reflects restricted water diffusion associated with the increased cellular density of malignant tissue.

The monoexponential model is, however, the simplest possible description of the DWI signal: it collapses the multi-b decay into a single scalar and discards information about the heterogeneous microstructure that generates it \citep{fennessy2023quantitative}.
Extended b-value protocols spanning $b = 0$ to $3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ reveal non-monoexponential decay arising from multiple diffusion compartments---restricted intracellular water, hindered extracellular diffusion, glandular luminal water, and vascular pseudo-diffusion \citep{lebihan1988separation, langkilde2018evaluation}.
A large literature has accordingly fit multi-compartment models---intravoxel incoherent motion \citep{lebihan1988separation}, biexponential \citep{mulkern2006biexponential} and multi-exponential \citep{conlin2021improved} decompositions, and biophysical models such as VERDICT \citep{panagiotaki2014microstructural}---to recover this detail.

Two facts sit in tension. First, despite being the crudest of these models, ADC is repeatedly hard to outperform for tumor \emph{detection}: across meta-analysis and fair head-to-head comparison, advanced models match but do not beat a properly localized, PI-RADS-compliant ADC \citep{si2018diagnostic, fennessy2023quantitative, he2025improved}, in part because their parameters are strongly correlated with ADC itself \citep{he2025improved}.
Second, recovering a diffusivity spectrum from a finite, noisy set of b-values is an ill-posed inverse problem \citep{prange2009quantifying, istratov1999exponential}: multi-compartment diffusion fits are known to be degenerate, fitting parameters the data may not constrain \citep{jelescu2016degeneracy, novikov2018modeling}, a hazard noted specifically for prostate multi-exponential analysis \citep{mulkern2017perils}.
Yet which compartments a realistic prostate acquisition can actually resolve has not been quantified, and the mechanism behind ADC's near-optimality for detection has not been rigorously explained---both questions a fully Bayesian treatment is well suited to answer.

We present a Bayesian spectral decomposition framework for multi-b prostate DWI and apply it to 56 patients (149 regions of interest).
Diffusivity spectra on a fixed eight-bin grid are estimated both by a maximum \emph{a posteriori} (MAP) point estimate and by full posterior inference with the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts}, the latter inferring the spectrum and the noise level jointly and yielding per-component posterior uncertainty \citep{sjolund2018bayesian, prange2009quantifying}.
We use this framework to (1)~quantify, by Fisher information and the Cram\'er--Rao bound and by the posterior itself, which diffusivity components are reliably recoverable; (2)~show that tumor-detection signal collapses onto two well-identified outer compartments that ADC already captures, mechanistically explaining its success; and (3)~demonstrate what the full posterior adds beyond a point estimate---propagated classification uncertainty, grade-dependent spectral shifts, and a per-voxel feasibility demonstration.
