\section{Introduction}

Diffusion-weighted magnetic resonance imaging (DWI) is a cornerstone of prostate cancer diagnosis, incorporated into the Prostate Imaging Reporting and Data System (PI-RADS) as a primary determinant of clinical significance \citep{turkbey2019prostate, weinreb2016pirads}.
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC), derived from monoexponential fitting of the DWI signal at two or more b-values, has become the most widely used quantitative biomarker for prostate cancer detection and grading \citep{manetta2019correlation, yan2024value}.
Lower ADC values indicate restricted water diffusion associated with increased cellular density in malignant tissue, and ADC has demonstrated strong discriminative performance for both tumor detection and Gleason grade differentiation \citep{donati2014prostate, rosenkrantz2015whole}.

However, the monoexponential ADC model collapses the multi-b signal decay into a single scalar, discarding information about the heterogeneous tissue microstructure that generates the observed signal \citep{fennessy2023quantitative}.
Extended b-value protocols spanning $b = 0$ to $3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ reveal non-monoexponential signal behavior arising from multiple diffusion compartments---including restricted intracellular water, hindered extracellular diffusion, glandular luminal water, and vascular pseudo-diffusion \citep{lebihan1988separation, langkilde2018evaluation}.
Multi-compartment models, including intravoxel incoherent motion (IVIM) \citep{lebihan1988separation}, biexponential fitting \citep{mulkern2006biexponential}, multi-exponential fitting \citep{conlin2021improved}, and biophysical models such as VERDICT \citep{panagiotaki2014microstructural} that additionally incorporate diffusion time and relaxation parameters, have been proposed to recover this microstructural detail.
\citet{conlin2021improved} demonstrated diffusion restriction imaging using four predefined diffusion coefficients, and \citet{langkilde2018evaluation} applied multi-component fitting to extended-range b-value prostate DWI, demonstrating that compartment-specific fractions provided complementary information to ADC for tissue characterization.

A fundamental challenge in multi-compartment diffusivity estimation is the ill-posedness of the inverse Laplace transform: recovering a spectrum of diffusivities from a finite, noisy set of b-value measurements is inherently underdetermined \citep{prange2009quantifying}.
Point estimation methods---including non-negative least squares and regularized regression---yield a single spectrum without quantifying how reliably each component is determined by the data.
Bayesian approaches address this limitation by sampling from the full posterior distribution, providing per-component uncertainty estimates that distinguish well-constrained spectral features from those that are effectively unconstrained by the measurements \citep{prange2009quantifying}.

In this work, we present a Bayesian spectral decomposition framework for multi-b prostate DWI and apply it to a cohort of 56 patients (149 regions of interest).
We estimate diffusivity spectra using both a maximum a~posteriori (MAP) point estimate and full Bayesian inference via the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts}.
We investigate (1)~how well each individual diffusivity component can be measured, both theoretically through Fisher information and the Cram\'er-Rao lower bound and practically through the NUTS posterior; (2)~how spectral classifiers compare with the conventional ADC for tumor detection and Gleason grade differentiation; and (3)~whether the near-equivalence of ADC and spectral classifiers can be explained mechanistically by an alignment between ADC's spectral sensitivity and the optimal classification direction.
We additionally demonstrate the framework at the pixel level on an independent patient dataset, producing spectral fraction maps, a spectral discriminant score, and per-voxel uncertainty estimates.
