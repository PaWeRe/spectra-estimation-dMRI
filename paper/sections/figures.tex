% --- Fig 1: Cohort Diffusivity Spectra by Tissue and Zone ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig1_v4.pdf}
\caption{%
Cohort diffusivity spectra by tissue type and anatomical zone.
Box plots show, for each diffusivity bin, the cohort distribution of the
estimated water fraction $R_j$ across ROIs: box edges at the 25th and 75th
percentiles, center line at the median, whiskers at 1.5$\times$ the
interquartile range, and outliers as individual points.
Top row: normal ROIs; bottom row: tumor ROIs.
Left column: peripheral zone (PZ); right column: transition zone (TZ).
Within each panel, MAP estimates (green, hatched) are plotted alongside NUTS
posterior-mean estimates (orange, solid).
Relative to normal tissue, tumor shows an elevated restricted-diffusion
fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and a reduced free-water fraction
($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) in both zones.
MAP and NUTS agree closely at the tuned regularization $\lambda = 10^{-3}$,
with only mild residual smoothing of the dominant free-water bin under MAP.
The grey numbers above each panel give the mean NUTS posterior coefficient of
variation (CV $=$ posterior std $/$ posterior mean) per bin, averaged over that
panel's ROIs, as a light indicator of per-component identifiability (developed
in Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}): the restricted ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and
free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) bins are well resolved (CV typically $< 0.4$),
whereas the intermediate bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) are poorly identified
(CV $> 0.7$) across all panels.
}
\label{fig:spectra}
\end{figure}

% --- Fig 3: ADC vs Spectral Discriminant ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig3_v3.pdf}
\caption{%
ADC is a near-optimal spectral discriminant, and the equivalence holds for both estimators.
ADC versus the spectral discriminant score (logistic regression linear
predictor from the eight spectral fractions) across ROIs, shown separately
for each anatomical zone (rows) and each spectral estimator (columns).
(Top)~peripheral zone (PZ; $n = 81$); (bottom)~transition zone (TZ; $n = 68$).
(Left)~NUTS posterior-mean features; (right)~constrained-MAP features at
tuned regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$).
All four panels share common axes; the discriminant is fit separately for
each zone and estimator, so its absolute score scale is comparable only
within a panel, whereas ADC ($x$-axis) is the same physical quantity throughout.
Tumor ROIs (red) cluster at low ADC and high discriminant; normal ROIs
(blue) at high ADC and low discriminant; the black line is the
ordinary-least-squares fit.
The per-panel Pearson correlations are PZ NUTS $r = -0.977$; PZ MAP
$r = -0.969$; TZ NUTS $r = -0.979$; TZ MAP $r = -0.959$.
The bracketed values are 95\% confidence intervals for $r$ obtained by a
percentile bootstrap: the ROIs within a panel are resampled with replacement
1000 times, $r$ is recomputed for each resample, and the interval is taken as
the 2.5th--97.5th percentile range of the resulting distribution; this
nonparametric interval avoids the distributional assumptions of the analytic
$p$-value, which is uninformative at $|r| > 0.95$ (see Results).
The near-perfect anti-correlation across all four panels demonstrates that
ADC rank-orders ROIs almost identically to the spectral classifier,
independent of whether the spectrum is estimated by closed-form MAP or by
full posterior sampling.
}
\label{fig:adc_discriminant}
\end{figure}

Normalized ADC sensitivity vector ($-\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$) and

% --- Fig 5: Average Diffusivity Spectrum by Gleason Grade Group ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig5_v2.pdf}
\caption{%
Average diffusivity spectrum stratified by Gleason Grade Group reveals a grade-dependent redistribution of spectral mass.
NUTS posterior-mean spectrum averaged across ROIs in each group: normal (gray, $n = 109$), low-grade tumor GGG\,$=\,1$ (blue, $n = 8$), and intermediate/high-grade tumor GGG\,$\geq 2$ (red, $n = 21$).
Shaded bands give the 95\% confidence interval of the group mean (Student's $t$-based, with $\text{df} = n - 1$), reflecting between-ROI variability of the per-ROI posterior mean; they do not include within-ROI posterior uncertainty.
The free-diffusion bin ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$), a proxy for intact gland lumen (free-water pools of $\sim$100--300\,\textmu m diameter), collapses from $0.39$ in normal tissue to $0.21$ in GGG\,$=\,1$ and $0.16$ in GGG\,$\geq 2$.
The intermediate bin at $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$, a candidate proxy for the glandular-epithelial compartment, is preserved in low-grade tumor ($0.28$, comparable to normal $0.26$) but reduced in higher-grade tumor ($0.20$); displaced mass redistributes primarily to the restricted-cellular bin at $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ ($0.07$ normal, $0.17$ GGG\,$=\,1$, $0.23$ GGG\,$\geq 2$).
Grade-specific sample sizes are small ($n = 8$ and $n = 21$) and the corresponding confidence bands are wide.
}
\label{fig:spectrum_ggg}
\end{figure}

\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig_fisher.pdf}
\caption{%
Fisher information analysis of the spectral discretization.
(a)~Fisher information correlation matrix: adjacent diffusivity components
($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) have mutual correlation $> 0.99$, making them
fundamentally indistinguishable from 15 b-value measurements.
$D = 0.25$ and $D = 20.0\;\umsmm$ are the most separable components.
(b)~Unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao lower bound (gray) versus observed NUTS
posterior standard deviation (orange) at SNR = 150.
Labels show the improvement factor.
Non-negativity constraints and Bayesian priors reduce estimation error by
100--8000$\times$, demonstrating their essential role.
(c)~Individual component decay curves $R_j \exp(-b D_j) / K$ at equal
fractions, with horizontal noise floors at SNR = 50--300.
Slow-diffusing components (e.g., $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) contribute signal
across all b-values; fast components ($D \geq 3.0\;\umsmm$) decay below the
noise floor within the first few b-value steps, motivating the sparser
discretization at high diffusivities.
}
\label{fig:fisher}
\end{figure}
