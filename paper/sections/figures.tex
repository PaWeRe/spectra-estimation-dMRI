\caption{%
Cohort diffusivity spectra by tissue type and anatomical zone.
Box plots show, for each diffusivity bin, the cohort distribution of the
estimated water fraction $R_j$ across ROIs: box edges at the 25th and 75th

% --- Fig 2: Tumor-detection ROC; collapse onto the two outer compartments ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig2_v1.pdf}
\caption{%
Tumor detection collapses onto the two outer diffusivity compartments, and the
spectral classifier is equivalent to ADC.
Leave-one-out cross-validated ROC curves for tumor-versus-normal classification
in the peripheral zone (PZ, left; $n = 81$) and transition zone (TZ, right;
$n = 68$).
For an apples-to-apples comparison, \emph{every} curve---including ADC and each
single component---is evaluated through the identical leave-one-out
logistic-regression pipeline ($C = 1.0$): a single monotonic feature incurs
negligible overfitting, whereas a multivariate model pays a finite-sample
penalty, so this removes the in-sample (raw-rank) versus held-out bias that
would otherwise flatter individual features.
Black: ADC. Orange: the spectral logistic-regression classifier built on all
eight fractions (solid) and on only the two outer fractions
$\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ (dashed).
Teal and brown: the single outer components $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ (restricted
diffusion) and $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ (free water).
Grey: the remaining six single components.
Across both zones the three thick curves are statistically indistinguishable
(overlapping bootstrap intervals; Table~\ref{tab:auc}), and the two-fraction
classifier matches or slightly exceeds the full eight-fraction one: adding the
six intermediate and free-water fractions does not improve detection and, at
this sample size, slightly degrades it.
The two outer single components are already strong individual detectors,
whereas the intermediate single components are weak---tumor-detection signal is
therefore concentrated in the two outer compartments, which is why a single
scalar (ADC) is difficult to improve upon.
AUC values with 95\% confidence intervals are reported in Table~\ref{tab:auc}.
}
\label{fig:roc}
\end{figure}

% --- Fig 3: ADC vs Spectral Discriminant ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig3_v3.pdf}
\caption{%
ADC is a near-optimal spectral discriminant, and the equivalence holds for both estimators.
ADC versus the spectral discriminant score (logistic regression linear
predictor from the eight spectral fractions) across ROIs, shown separately
for each anatomical zone (rows) and each spectral estimator (columns).
(Top)~peripheral zone (PZ; $n = 81$); (bottom)~transition zone (TZ; $n = 68$).
(Left)~NUTS posterior-mean features; (right)~constrained-MAP features at
tuned regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$).
All four panels share common axes; the discriminant is fit separately for
each zone and estimator, so its absolute score scale is comparable only
within a panel, whereas ADC ($x$-axis) is the same physical quantity throughout.
Tumor ROIs (red) cluster at low ADC and high discriminant; normal ROIs
(blue) at high ADC and low discriminant; the black line is the
ordinary-least-squares fit.
The per-panel Pearson correlations are PZ NUTS $r = -0.977$; PZ MAP
$r = -0.969$; TZ NUTS $r = -0.979$; TZ MAP $r = -0.959$.
The bracketed values are 95\% confidence intervals for $r$ obtained by a
percentile bootstrap: the ROIs within a panel are resampled with replacement
1000 times, $r$ is recomputed for each resample, and the interval is taken as
the 2.5th--97.5th percentile range of the resulting distribution; this
nonparametric interval avoids the distributional assumptions of the analytic
$p$-value, which is uninformative at $|r| > 0.95$ (see Results).
The near-perfect anti-correlation across all four panels demonstrates that
ADC rank-orders ROIs almost identically to the spectral classifier,
independent of whether the spectrum is estimated by closed-form MAP or by
full posterior sampling.
}
\label{fig:adc_discriminant}
\end{figure}

% --- Fig 4: per-bin anatomy of the spectral discriminant ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig4_v3.pdf}
\caption{%
The spectral discriminant is statistically a two-compartment detector, and its
two stable bins coincide with where ADC is most sensitive.
For each zone (PZ, TZ), bars give the standardized tumor-versus-normal
logistic-regression coefficient $w_{\mathrm{std}}$ per diffusivity bin --- the
same NUTS-feature fit as Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant} ($C = 1.0$, balanced,
standardized) --- normalized to $\max|w| = 1$, with error bars giving 95\%
confidence intervals from a nonparametric bootstrap (ROIs resampled with
replacement 2000 times, the logistic regression refit each time). Charcoal
diamonds give the ADC sensitivity $-\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ at the
average tumor spectrum (normalized to $\max|\cdot| = 1$, negated so positive
marks the tumor direction), with bootstrap CIs obtained by resampling the same
ROIs and recomputing the operating point; no connecting line is drawn because
the bins are discrete and unevenly spaced. Each bar is colored and hatched by
the bin's within-ROI posterior coefficient of variation (CV $=$ posterior std
$/$ mean, averaged over the zone's ROIs; colorblind-safe sequential, light/clean
$=$ well identified to dark/cross-hatched $=$ poorly identified, the same scheme
as the supplementary atlas), and the slim strip below each panel shows the same
per-bin CV as mean\,$\pm$\,std across ROIs. A star ($\ast$) marks the bins whose
coefficient CI excludes zero.
The star (cohort-level coefficient stability) and the bar colour (per-ROI
posterior identifiability) measure distinct reliabilities and need not agree:
a bin can carry a reproducible tumor-versus-normal weight across the cohort
(starred) while remaining poorly resolved within any single ROI (dark), because
averaging over ROIs suppresses the per-scan noise that drives the CV.
In both zones the two outer bins carry stable weight: $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ a
positive weight and $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ a negative weight (each 100\% sign-stable
across bootstrap resamples). In the peripheral zone no other bin is
distinguishable from zero; in the transition zone two intermediate bins
($D = 0.75,\,1.0\;\umsmm$) marginally exclude zero but are small and lie in the
poorly-identified band (CV $\approx 0.8$), a faint residual signal we do not
over-interpret. The instability of the remaining intermediate coefficients is a
consequence of inter-bin collinearity compounded by poor identifiability, and
means their \emph{individual} detection weights are not separately attributable
--- not that the bins are uninformative. For detection the discriminant therefore
reduces, to good approximation, to the two outer compartments, which is exactly
where ADC is most sensitive (with the matching opposite sign): this collapse is
why a single scalar (ADC) is hard to improve upon. The intermediate bins' value
appears on a separate axis: Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg} shows the intermediate
and lumen fractions shift with Gleason grade (a univariate grading signal),
which is why we still report the full spectrum rather than only the two outer
bins. Overall the coefficient profile anti-correlates with
$\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ at Pearson $r = -0.79$ (PZ; bootstrap 95\% CI
$[-0.89, -0.50]$) and $r = -0.88$ (TZ; $[-0.95, -0.70]$); this whole-profile
correlation is carried by the two outer bins and is weaker, and less certain,
than the near-unity value reported under heavy regularization
($\lambda = 0.1$), where smearing of mass into the intermediate bins
artificially smooths the profile (Methods; Discussion). The negligible weight on
$D = 20\;\umsmm$ reflects ADC's insensitivity to the vascular compartment: its
signal has decayed to near zero at all $b > 0$, leaving only the intercept to
constrain it \citep{aksitciris2019accelerated}.
}
\label{fig:sensitivity}
\end{figure}

% --- Fig 5: Average Diffusivity Spectrum by Gleason Grade Group ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig5_v3.pdf}
\caption{%
Two Gleason-grade thresholds dissociate a detection axis from a grading axis in the diffusivity spectrum.
NUTS posterior-mean spectrum averaged across ROIs, with normal tissue (gray, $n = 109$) as the shared baseline in both panels.
Shaded bands give the 95\% confidence interval of the group mean (Student's $t$-based, $\text{df} = n - 1$), reflecting between-ROI variability of the per-ROI posterior mean; they do not include within-ROI posterior uncertainty.
(Left)~tumor \emph{emergence}: low-grade GGG\,$=\,1$ (blue, $n = 8$) versus all clinically significant tumor GGG\,$\geq 2$ (red, $n = 21$).
(Right)~tumor \emph{aggressiveness}: favorable-grade GGG\,$\leq 2$ (blue, $n = 20$) versus unfavorable-grade GGG\,$\geq 3$ (red, $n = 9$).
The free-diffusion bin ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$), a proxy for intact gland lumen (free-water pools of $\sim$100--300\,\textmu m diameter), collapses from $0.39$ in normal tissue to $0.21$ as soon as tumor is present (GGG\,$=\,1$) and changes little thereafter ($0.16$ at GGG\,$\geq 2$; $0.18$ versus $0.16$ across the aggressiveness threshold): this outer free-water compartment saturates at tumor onset and carries detection, not grade.
In contrast, the restricted-cellular bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises monotonically with grade ($0.07$ normal, $0.17$ GGG\,$=\,1$, $0.23$ GGG\,$\geq 2$, $0.31$ GGG\,$\geq 3$), and the glandular-epithelial proxy ($D = 2.0\;\umsmm$) is preserved in low-grade tumor ($0.28$ at GGG\,$=\,1$, comparable to normal $0.26$) but is progressively depleted with grade ($0.20$ at GGG\,$\geq 2$, $0.17$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$): these restricted and intermediate compartments---where ADC is least sensitive---track aggressiveness.
Grade-specific sample sizes are small (down to $n = 9$ for GGG\,$\geq 3$) and the corresponding confidence bands are wide.
}
\label{fig:spectrum_ggg}
\end{figure}

% --- Fig 6: Uncertainty-aware classifier (posterior propagation) ---
\begin{figure}[p]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig6_v1.pdf}
\caption{%
Propagating the diffusivity-spectrum posterior through the classifier turns a
binary label into a calibrated probability of cancer, whose uncertainty is
largest where the classifier is most likely to be wrong.
For each ROI the leave-one-out logistic-regression classifier---the same
NUTS-feature tumor-versus-normal pipeline as Figure~\ref{fig:roc}
($C = 1$)---is applied not to a single feature vector but to every NUTS
posterior draw of the eight spectral fractions, yielding a posterior
\emph{distribution} over the cancer probability $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ rather than
a point estimate.
(Top)~Per-ROI predictions in the peripheral zone (PZ; $n = 81$) and transition
zone (TZ; $n = 68$), sorted by predicted probability: markers give
$P(\mathrm{tumor})$ at the posterior-mean spectrum (blue circles, normal; red
squares, tumor) and error bars the propagated 90\% credible interval; black
rings mark misclassified ROIs.
The propagated intervals are widest near the decision boundary and pinch toward
confident predictions (partly a geometric property of the logistic link; see
Discussion).
(Bottom left)~Pooled across zones, the 90\% credible interval is on average
$2.4\times$ wider for misclassified than for correctly classified ROIs
(mean width $0.39$ vs $0.16$; Welch $p = 0.003$); box edges at quartiles,
whiskers at $1.5\times$ the interquartile range, points colored by true tissue.
The residual confidently-misclassified ROIs (narrow interval, far from the
boundary) are almost all low-grade (GGG\,$=\,1$) or ungraded tumors whose
spectra are indistinguishable from normal tissue---a limit of diffusion
contrast for low-grade disease (cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}), not a
failure of the uncertainty estimate.
A MAP point estimate cannot produce these intervals; the per-bin posterior from
full Bayesian sampling is what makes such an uncertainty-aware classifier
possible.
This analysis is exploratory: it illustrates a capability of the Bayesian
posterior rather than a validated diagnostic.
}
\label{fig:uncertainty}
\end{figure}

\caption{%
Validation of the joint Bayesian spectral inference on simulated ground truth, at the cohort-median SNR of 303.
\textbf{(a,b)}~Recovery of a normal-like and a tumour-like ground-truth spectrum (constructed from the cohort-mean NUTS spectra; horizontal black marks denote the truth at each bin). For a single noisy realisation, the NUTS posterior (orange) and a Gibbs posterior (slate) are drawn as box plots (box = interquartile range, whiskers = 5th--95th percentile, line = median), with the tuned-MAP point estimate ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$) as a green cross. All three estimators recover the realistic spectra; the wide posteriors at the intermediate bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) reflect the genuine identifiability limit of this b-value grid.
\textbf{(c)}~A concentrated $\delta$ stress test (all mass at $D = 0.75\;\umsmm$): the spike is under-recovered and smeared into the correlated neighbouring bins, showing that recovery is non-trivial (not a self-fulfilling reconstruction) and quantifying the worst-case bias.
\textbf{(d)}~Joint noise inference: the inferred noise level $\hat{\sigma}$ across 25 independent noise realisations per ground truth (NUTS posterior mean) versus the true $\sigma = 1/\mathrm{SNR}$ (dashed). The noise level is recovered to within $\sim$10\% on the realistic spectra and is over-estimated on the pathological $\delta$ case.
\textbf{(e)}~Sampler mixing on the $\delta$ stress case: per-bin effective sample size per draw (log scale). NUTS mixes uniformly well, whereas the coordinate-wise Gibbs sampler's efficiency collapses by $\sim$2--3 orders of magnitude on the mutually-correlated intermediate bins.
\emph{Configurations.} NUTS: half-normal prior $\sigma_R = 1/\sqrt{\lambda} = 3.16$ ($\lambda = 0.1$), $\sigma \sim$~half-Cauchy$(\beta = 1/\mathrm{SNR})$ inferred jointly with the spectrum, 2000 draws, 200 tuning steps, 4 chains, target acceptance 0.95. Gibbs: identical ridge prior, $\sigma$ fixed at $1/\mathrm{SNR}$, truncated-normal full conditionals, $10^{5}$ iterations, $10^{4}$ burn-in, 4 chains. MAP: constrained ridge NNLS, $\lambda = 10^{-3}$. Both samplers initialised at the MAP estimate.
