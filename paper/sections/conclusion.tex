\section{Conclusion}
Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate DWI reveals that ADC is a near-optimal spectral discriminant for tumor detection, with ADC scores anti-correlating with the optimal eight-component classifier at $r \approx -0.98$.
The per-bin analysis shows that this near-optimality arises because tumor-detection signal concentrates on the restricted-diffusion and free-water compartments---which are precisely the compartments to which ADC is most sensitive; the alignment between the discriminant and ADC's spectral sensitivity is moderate but real.
Fisher information analysis shows that the inverse problem is severely ill-conditioned ($\kappa = 7.7 \times 10^{10}$), with intermediate diffusivity components ($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) fundamentally indistinguishable at typical clinical noise levels and even at the much lower noise levels realized for the present cohort acquired with an endorectal coil and low spatial resolution.
Bayesian inference combines the prior with the non-negativity constraint to reduce the effective estimation error by up to three orders of magnitude relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound, making the spectral decomposition tractable and identifying restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, CV = 0.20) and free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, CV = 0.32) as the only well-resolved components.
MAP and NUTS yield equivalent discrimination but complementary information: MAP provides fast, stable point estimates; NUTS reveals what the data can and cannot resolve.
Pixel-wise spectral mapping demonstrates the feasibility of voxel-level tissue characterization with intrinsic uncertainty estimates.
