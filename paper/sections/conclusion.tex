\section{Conclusion}

Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate DWI reveals that ADC is a near-optimal spectral discriminant for tumor detection, with ADC scores anti-correlating with the optimal eight-component classifier at $r \approx -0.98$.
The ADC sensitivity analysis confirms that this near-optimality arises from an implicit alignment between ADC's spectral weighting and the learned classification direction.
Fisher information analysis shows that the inverse problem is severely ill-conditioned ($\kappa = 7.7 \times 10^{10}$), with intermediate diffusivity components ($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) fundamentally indistinguishable at clinical noise levels. Bayesian inference with non-negativity priors reduces effective estimation error by two to three orders of magnitude relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound, making the spectral decomposition tractable and identifying restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, CV = 0.20) and free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, CV = 0.32) as the only well-resolved components.
MAP and NUTS yield equivalent discrimination but complementary information: MAP provides fast, stable point estimates; NUTS reveals what the data can and cannot resolve.
Pixel-wise spectral mapping demonstrates the feasibility of voxel-level tissue characterization with intrinsic uncertainty estimates.
