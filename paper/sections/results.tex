\subsection{Tumor detection}

Table~\ref{tab:auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:roc} summarize detection performance.
ADC was the strongest single detector (raw-rank AUC PZ $0.951$, TZ $0.979$), and the spectral classifiers matched it: through the identical leave-one-out pipeline the eight-fraction logistic regression reached PZ $0.926$ / TZ $0.923$ (NUTS) and PZ $0.917$ / TZ $0.952$ (MAP), with bootstrap confidence intervals overlapping ADC's throughout.

I would think a fair comparison would be to use the same weights as ADC automatically does.); the in-sample ADC raw rank is reported as the hardest reference baseline.
Restricting the classifier to the restricted and free-water fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ did not reduce performance (NUTS PZ $0.933$, TZ $0.937$; MAP TZ $0.965$). Conversely, classifying on the five intermediate fractions alone---the restricted, free-water, and $D = 20\;\umsmm$ vascular pseudo-diffusion fractions removed---dropped NUTS detection to PZ $0.818$ / TZ $0.796$ (Table~\ref{tab:auc}), pinning down where the detection signal lives. Detection is thus both carried and bounded by the restricted and free-water compartments; the intermediate fractions add only finite-sample noise once those are present, which is why the two-feature model matches, and sometimes slightly exceeds, the eight-feature one.
This reflects redundancy rather than uselessness. The individual-fraction AUCs (Table~\ref{tab:auc}) show the restricted and free-water bins are the strongest single detectors (AUC $0.88$--$0.92$), but several intermediate fractions also discriminate on their own (up to $\sim$0.85, varying by zone), and even the four most poorly identified bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.5\;\umsmm$, posterior CV $> 0.7$) reach AUC $0.82$ (PZ) / $0.81$ (TZ) when used together. The weakest single bins are zone-dependent: $D = 20\;\umsmm$ is near chance in both zones (AUC $0.52$/$0.55$), while $D = 1.5$ and $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ trade places---$D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ is near chance in PZ ($0.59$) but discriminates in TZ ($0.79$), and $D = 1.5\;\umsmm$ the reverse is true (PZ $0.83$, TZ $0.55$)---these being the collinear intermediate bins whose weight is poorly resolved within each ROI's posterior (Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}). The spectrum is therefore not split into informative and uninformative bins: the intermediate fractions co-vary with the dominant tumor axis, so each is individually informative yet contributes nothing once the restricted and free-water compartments are present.
Detection AUCs varied by $< 0.03$ across $C = 0.1$--$10$. Grade-classification AUCs are not reported---the $n = 29$ graded tumors are too few for a reliable estimate---and grade-dependent spectral changes are taken up in the Discussion section.

\subsection{ADC equals the spectral discriminant}

Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant---the trained logistic-regression score applied to each ROI's spectrum---in both zones and for both spectral estimators (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}). Ranking ROIs by ADC is therefore almost identical to ranking them by the optimal spectral classifier: using NUTS posterior-mean features at $C = 1.0$, the Spearman rank correlation---the measure relevant to detection, which depends only on how ROIs are ranked---was $\rho = -0.979$ (PZ, $n = 81$) and $\rho = -0.977$ (TZ, $n = 68$); the Pearson correlation, quantifying the linear near-identity, was $-0.977$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.985,\,-0.964]$) and $-0.981$ ($[-0.988,\,-0.971]$). Constrained-MAP features gave equivalent values (Spearman PZ $-0.980$, TZ $-0.929$; Pearson $-0.972$, $-0.967$). The near-identity was robust across regularization strengths ($C = 0.1$ to $10$) and held for both MAP and NUTS, indicating that it is a property of the data rather than of any particular classifier setting.

To test the separability bound of Eq.~\ref{eq:dmax}, we estimated each zone's class-conditional covariance by Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage (@Patrick: investigate method and check if right choice here, don't make too complex for readers) of the NUTS posterior-mean spectra and computed the in-sample Mahalanobis ceiling on detection.
The achievable AUC was $0.96$ (PZ) and $0.99$ (TZ), and ADC came within $0.01$ of it (raw-rank $0.951$/$0.979$, above)---ADC thus captures $\cos^2\theta \approx 0.88$ (PZ) / $0.82$ (TZ) of the available squared separability (Eq.~\ref{eq:cos}) while losing almost no AUC, the shortfall absorbed by the saturation of $\Phi$ near the ceiling.
Replacing the full covariance with the estimation covariance $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{est}}$ alone---removing the biological heterogeneity $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{bio}}$ and leaving only posterior measurement error---raised the ceiling to AUC $\approx 1.0$.
Detection is therefore biology-limited, not measurement-limited: the residual gap to perfect separation is irreducible biological overlap between tumor and normal spectra rather than estimation noise, so a richer inversion of the same acquisition---which can only shrink $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{est}}$---cannot improve detection, and raising the ceiling would require new contrast.
