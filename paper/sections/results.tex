\subsection{Pixel-wise Spectral Decomposition}

Pixel-wise spectral estimation was performed on 146 manually segmented prostate voxels
from patient 8640 (slice~6).
Both MAP (Ridge NNLS, $\lambda=0.1$) and fully Bayesian (NUTS, 2000 draws, 4 chains)
estimates were computed for all voxels.
All 146 NUTS runs converged ($\hat{R} = 1.000$).
Estimated signal-to-noise ratios ranged from 12 to 172 across voxels
($\text{SNR} = 1/\hat{\sigma}$, where $\hat{\sigma}$ is the posterior mean of the
noise parameter).

\subsubsection{Spectral Fraction Maps}

Figure~\ref{fig:pixelwise} shows pixel-wise maps of selected spectral fractions alongside
the conventional ADC map.
The restricted diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) showed the strongest spatial
contrast (spatial coefficient of variation $\text{CV} = 0.61$), exceeding that of ADC
($\text{CV} = 0.26$) and providing a more specific marker of the restricted diffusion
compartment.
The free-water fraction ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) showed an approximately inverse spatial
pattern, consistent with larger extracellular spaces in normal glandular tissue.

\subsubsection{MAP and NUTS Estimates Compared}

For the clinically relevant restricted diffusion component ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$),
MAP and NUTS posterior means were highly correlated ($r = 0.986$) with NUTS
estimates running approximately 17\% higher on average.
In terms of in-sample signal reconstruction (RMSE between observed and predicted
normalized signal), NUTS posterior means achieved lower residuals for 91\% of
voxels overall and 100\% of high-SNR voxels ($\text{SNR} > 80$).
This is expected in part because the MAP estimate trades data fidelity for
regularization through the Ridge penalty, whereas NUTS marginalizes over the
noise parameter and samples from the full posterior.
Better data fit does not necessarily imply a more accurate spectrum in the
absence of ground truth; the primary advantage of the fully Bayesian approach
lies in the uncertainty estimates rather than the point estimates.

For intermediate diffusivity components ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$), MAP and NUTS
estimates diverged substantially ($r = 0.27$--$0.65$), reflecting poor identifiability
of adjacent spectral bins from 15 b-value measurements.
This was confirmed by the NUTS posterior: the coefficient of variation (CV = posterior
standard deviation / posterior mean) for these components was 0.75--0.81, indicating
that the uncertainty was comparable to the estimate itself.
In contrast, $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ had $\text{CV} = 0.17$, identifying it as the
best-constrained spectral component.

\subsubsection{Spectral Discriminant Score}

To synthesize the multi-component spectral information into a single spatial biomarker,
we computed a spectral discriminant score at each voxel.
Logistic regression coefficients were trained on ROI-level spectral profiles
(81 peripheral zone samples: 27 tumor, 54 normal; LOOCV AUC = 0.919) and applied
unchanged to pixel-level normalized spectra:
%
\begin{equation}
  \text{disc}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{8} w_j \, \hat{R}_j(\mathbf{x}),
  \label{eq:discriminant}
\end{equation}
%
where $w_j$ are the fixed logistic regression coefficients and $\hat{R}_j(\mathbf{x})$
is the normalized spectral fraction at voxel~$\mathbf{x}$.
Positive coefficients ($w_j > 0$ for $D \leq 1.0\;\umsmm$) indicate spectral
components associated with tumor tissue (restricted diffusion), while negative
coefficients ($w_j < 0$ for $D \geq 1.5\;\umsmm$) indicate components associated
with normal tissue (free water and glandular diffusion).

The resulting spectral discriminant map was near-perfectly anti-correlated with ADC
($r = -0.971$), demonstrating that ADC can be understood as a special case of the
spectral discriminant---a particular weighted sum of spectral fractions.
However, the spectral decomposition additionally reveals \emph{which tissue compartments}
drive the observed ADC value at each voxel, providing biological interpretability that
ADC alone cannot offer.

\subsubsection{Uncertainty-Aware Discriminant}

The fully Bayesian approach enables propagation of posterior uncertainty through the
discriminant score.
For each voxel, 500 Monte Carlo samples were drawn from the approximate NUTS posterior
(Gaussian with per-component mean and standard deviation), normalized, and passed through
Eq.~\ref{eq:discriminant}.
The resulting uncertainty in the discriminant score (standard deviation of MC samples)
ranged from 0.060 to 0.140 across voxels (mean = 0.094), representing 13--19\% of the
total discriminant range ($[-0.27, +0.48]$).
This uncertainty map identifies voxels where the spectral tumor/normal classification is
least reliable and provides an intrinsic quality metric that requires no external
reference standard.
