\subsection{Tumor detection}

Table~\ref{tab:auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:roc} summarize detection performance.
ADC was the strongest single detector (raw-rank AUC PZ $0.951$, TZ $0.979$), and the spectral classifiers matched it: through the identical leave-one-out pipeline the eight-fraction logistic regression reached PZ $0.926$ / TZ $0.923$ (NUTS) and PZ $0.917$ / TZ $0.952$ (MAP), with bootstrap confidence intervals overlapping ADC's throughout.
The fair head-to-head is ADC versus the spectral models scored through the same held-out pipeline; the in-sample ADC raw rank is reported as the hardest reference baseline.
Restricting the classifier to the two outer fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ did not reduce performance (NUTS PZ $0.933$, TZ $0.937$; MAP TZ $0.965$): the six intermediate fractions carry no detection signal beyond the two outer compartments, so in the held-out pipeline they add only finite-sample noise---which is why the two-feature model matches, and sometimes slightly exceeds, the eight-feature one.
The individual-fraction AUCs (Table~\ref{tab:auc}) refine this picture: the two outer bins are the strongest single detectors (AUC $0.88$--$0.92$), but several intermediate fractions also discriminate on their own (up to $\sim$0.85, varying by zone), so the spectrum is not cleanly split into informative and uninformative bins---these fractions co-vary with the dominant tumor axis and simply add nothing once the two outer compartments are present.
Grade-classification AUCs are not reported ($n = 29$ graded tumors are too imprecise; see Table~\ref{tab:auc}); grading is instead presented below as grade-dependent spectral shifts.
Detection AUCs varied by $< 0.03$ across $C = 0.1$--$10$.

\subsection{Why ADC works}

Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant---the trained logistic-regression score applied to each ROI's spectrum---in both zones and for both spectral estimators (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}). Ranking ROIs by ADC is therefore almost identical to ranking them by the optimal spectral classifier: using NUTS posterior-mean features at $C = 1.0$, the Spearman rank correlation was $\rho = -0.979$ (PZ, $n = 81$) and $\rho = -0.977$ (TZ, $n = 68$), with Pearson correlations of $-0.977$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.985,\,-0.964]$) and $-0.981$ ($[-0.988,\,-0.971]$). Constrained-MAP features gave equivalent values (Spearman PZ $-0.980$, TZ $-0.929$; Pearson $-0.972$, $-0.967$). The near-identity was robust across regularization strengths ($C = 0.1$ to $10$) and held for both MAP and NUTS, indicating that it is a property of the data rather than of any particular classifier setting.

The mechanism is visible in the per-bin structure of the discriminant (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).
The ADC sensitivity vector $\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ at the average tumor spectrum is strongly negative for restricted diffusion ($-1.59$: a larger restricted fraction lowers ADC) and positive for free water ($+0.85$); because the logistic-regression coefficients point the opposite way, Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} plots the sign-flipped $-\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ alongside them, so aligned bars indicate matching direction.
Using NUTS features, the two vectors align well (Pearson $r = -0.79$, PZ, $95\%$ CI $[-0.89,\,-0.50]$; $r = -0.88$, TZ, $[-0.95,\,-0.70]$), and the alignment is carried by the two outer bins: only $D = 0.25$ and $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ have logistic coefficients whose bootstrap intervals exclude zero.
ADC therefore weights the spectrum much as the optimal classifier does, concentrating on the two well-identified compartments---which is why the two scores rank ROIs almost identically; the moderate profile correlation reflects only the noisy intermediate coefficients, which carry little weight in either method.

\subsection{Prediction confidence}

Each classifier returns a probability $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ rather than a hard label, and misclassified ROIs concentrate near the decision boundary ($P \approx 0.5$): ranking predictions by their distance from the boundary separates likely errors from confident, correct calls, so the classifier flags its own least-reliable predictions (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}).
This confidence is a property of the logistic model, available from the point estimate alone, and is shared by the ADC- and spectrum-based classifiers.
Propagating the full NUTS posterior through the classifier---applying it to every posterior draw rather than the posterior mean---does not add to this.
The 90\% credible interval on $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ is on average $2.4\times$ wider for misclassified than for correctly classified ROIs (pooled across zones, mean width $0.39$ vs $0.16$), but this reflects the logistic geometry rather than the spectral posterior: the slope $P(1-P)$ is largest at the boundary, so in logit space the difference falls to $\approx 1.3\times$ and is no longer significant, and the interval width does not predict misclassification once distance to the boundary is accounted for.
Offering the per-component posterior standard deviations to the classifier as features likewise leaves detection AUC essentially unchanged.
The posterior therefore quantifies which spectral components are reliably estimated (identifiability), but adds no diagnostic information to the tumor-versus-normal decision beyond the point estimate.
