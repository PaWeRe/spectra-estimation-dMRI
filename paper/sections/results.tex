\section{Results}

\subsection{Tissue-Specific Diffusivity Spectra}

The trace-decay signal-to-noise ratio ($S_0/\sigma$ from the per-ROI NUTS posterior) had a median of 303 across the 149~ROIs (interquartile range 176--478; full range 25--1548), the wide spread reflecting tissue composition and distance from the endorectal coil.
Figure~\ref{fig:spectra} shows the MAP and NUTS spectral fractions averaged across ROIs of each tissue type.
Tumor ROIs had an elevated restricted-diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$: 18.5\% MAP / 16.6\% NUTS in PZ tumor vs.\ 7.2\% / 5.7\% in PZ normal) and a reduced free-water fraction ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$: 18.9\% / 21.0\% in PZ tumor vs.\ 40.3\% / 47.5\% in PZ normal), consistent with increased cellular density restricting water mobility.
The transition zone showed the same pattern, with a higher restricted fraction overall in TZ tumor ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$: 26.4\% / 25.0\% vs.\ 9.9\% / 8.5\% in TZ normal).
At the tuned regularization, MAP and NUTS agree closely on the two well-identified outer compartments.

\subsection{What the Data Can Resolve}

Identifiability is set primarily by the acquisition, not the estimator. The Fisher-information correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a) exceeds $0.99$ among the components at $D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$---a property of the b-value grid that is independent of how large each fraction is---so these components are intrinsically hard to separate. The posterior confirms this directly: the restricted ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) compartments have the smallest absolute posterior standard deviations relative to their mass (0.015 and 0.078) and the lowest coefficients of variation (CV $= 0.20$ and $0.32$), whereas the glandular compartment at $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$---the second-largest fraction in the spectrum---has the \emph{largest} absolute posterior uncertainty (0.083 at $D = 1.5$, 0.121 at $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$). Because this large uncertainty accompanies a large fraction, it is a genuine data limit rather than a small-denominator artifact.
The CV is correspondingly high for the intermediate bins (0.74--0.83), but for the low-mass slow components ($D = 0.5$--$0.75\;\umsmm$, mean fraction $\approx 0.04$) part of that ratio simply reflects their small means; we therefore read identifiability from the Fisher structure and the absolute posterior width, with CV as a secondary, relative descriptor.
This data limit is robust to prior width (varying the half-normal scale up to $30\times$ left intermediate-bin uncertainty essentially unchanged) and is reproduced in simulation (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}): the sampler recovers smooth spectra accurately at the cohort SNR and jointly recovers $\sigma$ to within $\sim$10\%, but cannot localize a concentrated $\delta$-spike to a single intermediate bin, smearing it across the correlated neighbors instead.
Crucially, ``poorly identified'' is not ``irrelevant'': the glandular bins remain physiologically meaningful and, as shown below, carry grade information even though their fractions cannot be pinned down precisely.

\subsection{Tumor Detection Collapses onto Two Compartments}

Table~\ref{tab:auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:roc} summarize detection performance.
ADC was the strongest single detector (raw rank PZ $0.951$ [$0.89$, $1.00$]; TZ $0.979$ [$0.94$, $1.00$]), but the spectral classifiers were statistically indistinguishable from it, with overlapping bootstrap intervals throughout: the eight-fraction logistic regression reached PZ $0.926$ [$0.85$, $0.98$] (NUTS) / $0.917$ (MAP) and TZ $0.923$ (NUTS) / $0.952$ (MAP).
Restricting the classifier to the two outer fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ \emph{matched} the full eight-fraction model (NUTS PZ $0.933$, TZ $0.937$; MAP TZ $0.965$): the two-fraction and eight-fraction AUCs differ only within finite-sample cross-validation noise, so the six remaining fractions carry no detection signal beyond the two outer compartments, for both estimators.
Among the individual fractions, the two outer bins were the strongest single detectors, but several intermediate bins were also individually informative; only $D = 20\;\umsmm$ (both zones), $D = 1.5\;\umsmm$ (TZ), and $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ (PZ) were near chance (Figure~\ref{fig:roc}).
The detection signal is thus low-dimensional---essentially the coupled motion of the two outer compartments---which is the empirical basis for the equivalence to ADC examined next.

\subsection{Why ADC Works: a Near-Optimal Spectral Discriminant}

Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant score in both zones and for both estimators (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}): NUTS PZ $r = -0.977$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.985,\,-0.964]$), TZ $r = -0.979$ ($[-0.988,\,-0.971]$); constrained-MAP PZ $r = -0.969$, TZ $r = -0.959$. Ranking ROIs by ADC is therefore almost identical to ranking them by the optimal eight-component classifier, independent of estimator.
The per-bin anatomy explains why (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}). The ADC sensitivity $\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ at the average tumor spectrum is strongly negative for restricted diffusion ($-1.59$) and positive for free water ($+0.85$); the logistic-regression coefficients point oppositely, and only the two outer bins carry coefficient weight whose bootstrap intervals exclude zero, while the intermediate coefficients are individually unstable from inter-bin collinearity.
The whole-profile alignment between the discriminant and the (sign-flipped) ADC sensitivity is moderate but clear---$r = -0.79$ (PZ; $[-0.89,\,-0.50]$) and $-0.88$ (TZ; $[-0.95,\,-0.70]$)---and is carried by the two outer bins. ADC is, in effect, reading out the same two-compartment contrast the optimal classifier uses, which is why a single scalar suffices for detection.

\subsection{Beyond Detection: Uncertainty, Grade, and Voxel-level Feasibility}

The following analyses use capabilities unique to the full posterior and are exploratory.

\emph{Propagated classification uncertainty (exploratory).}
Because NUTS yields a full posterior over each ROI's spectrum, applying the trained classifier to every posterior draw turns each prediction into a distribution over the cancer probability $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ with a credible interval rather than a single label (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty})---a calibrated probability a point estimate cannot produce.
The interval is wider for misclassified than correctly classified ROIs ($2.4\times$ in probability space; patient-level bootstrap CI $[1.7,3.4]$), but this is largely the geometry of the logistic link, which stretches probability intervals near $P = 0.5$: in logit space, which removes that geometry, the boundary correlation falls from $\rho = -0.94$ to $-0.29$ and the misclassified-versus-correct ratio falls to $1.3\times$ with a bootstrap CI of $[0.94,1.67]$ that includes one. Deferring the widest-interval ROIs improves accuracy mostly because it defers cases near the decision boundary---information the point-estimate probability already carries---so in this cohort the propagated interval does not add a statistically reliable error flag beyond the point estimate. \emph{Grade-dependent spectral shifts.}
Resolving tumor by Gleason Grade Group (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}) dissociates a detection axis from a grading axis.
The free-water bin ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, intact gland lumen) is suppressed as soon as tumor appears ($0.39$ normal $\to 0.21$ at GGG~$=1$) and then changes little ($0.16$ at GGG~$\geq 3$): it separates tumor from normal but not grade from grade.
By contrast the restricted-cellular bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises with grade ($0.07 \to 0.17 \to 0.31$) and the glandular bin ($D = 2.0\;\umsmm$) is preserved in low-grade then depleted ($0.26$ normal, $0.28$ at GGG~$=1$, $0.17$ at GGG~$\geq 3$)---bins where ADC is least sensitive (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).
The grading signal therefore lives in exactly the intermediate compartments the data resolve least precisely, which is why we present it as a hypothesis-generating observation: grade groups are small (down to $n = 8$--$9$), and 11 of 40 tumor ROIs (7 with GGG~$=0$, 4 ungraded) are not stratified. \stephan{should the GGG~$=0$ tumor ROIs join the normal split, or are they a distinct category? please advise.} Re-running the averaging on tuned-MAP estimates gave indistinguishable trajectories on all eight bins.

\emph{Pixel-wise feasibility.}
Applied voxel-wise on a single slice (Figure~\ref{fig:pixelwise}), the framework reproduces ADC ($r = -0.93$ for the two-bin and $-0.96$ for the eight-bin discriminant) while adding what point estimates cannot: a per-voxel uncertainty map, on which tumor regions are among the more certain (supporting confidence in flagged disease) and the eight-bin score is $\sim$$1.8\times$ more uncertain than the two-bin score because it weights the unidentifiable intermediate bins. The absolute score skews tumor-like at the voxel level---the lower SNR of a single voxel inflates the restricted fraction---which is why all quantitative results are reported at the ROI level (Discussion).
Direction independence was confirmed on the separate three-direction acquisition (Figure~\ref{fig:directions}): the per-direction CV was lowest for the diagnostically dominant restricted-diffusion bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, mean $9\%$) and large only on the near-empty bins, where it is a small-denominator effect rather than anisotropy, confirming that trace averaging is appropriate.
