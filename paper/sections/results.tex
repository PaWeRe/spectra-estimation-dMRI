\section{Results}

\subsection{Diffusivity spectra}

Figure~\ref{fig:spectra} shows the cohort MAP and NUTS spectra by tissue type and zone, with per-ROI spectra in the supplementary atlas (Figure~\ref{fig:atlas}); MAP and NUTS agreed closely throughout.
From normal to tumor, both zones gained mass at $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ at the expense of $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ (and, in the transition zone, $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ as well).
The normal baseline itself differed by zone: peripheral-zone normal tissue was dominated by $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ (mean $0.48$) with a wide cohort spread, whereas transition-zone normal tissue placed more, and less variable, mass on the $D = 2.0$ and $3.0\;\umsmm$ bins (each $\approx 0.29$).
Tumor spectra were likewise zone-dependent: the $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ fraction was both higher and far more variable across the cohort in transition-zone tumor than in transition-zone normal ($0.25 \pm 0.14$ vs.\ $0.09 \pm 0.03$), while peripheral-zone tumor retained more $D = 2.0$--$3.0\;\umsmm$ mass than transition-zone tumor.
The grade-dependent reading of these shifts is deferred to the Discussion.

\subsection{Tumor detection}

Table~\ref{tab:auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:roc} summarize detection performance.
ADC was the strongest single detector (raw-rank AUC PZ $0.951$, TZ $0.979$), and the spectral classifiers matched it: through the identical leave-one-out pipeline the eight-fraction logistic regression reached PZ $0.926$ / TZ $0.923$ (NUTS) and PZ $0.917$ / TZ $0.952$ (MAP), with bootstrap confidence intervals overlapping ADC's throughout.
The fair head-to-head is ADC versus the spectral models scored through the same held-out pipeline; the in-sample ADC raw rank is reported as the hardest reference baseline.
Restricting the classifier to the two outer fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ did not reduce performance (NUTS PZ $0.933$, TZ $0.937$; MAP TZ $0.965$). The mirror ablation pins down where the signal lives: classifying on the six inner fractions alone---the two outer compartments removed---dropped NUTS detection to PZ $0.817$ / TZ $0.775$ (Table~\ref{tab:auc}). Detection is thus both carried and bounded by the two outer compartments; the inner fractions add only finite-sample noise once those are present, which is why the two-feature model matches, and sometimes slightly exceeds, the eight-feature one.
This reflects redundancy rather than uselessness. The individual-fraction AUCs (Table~\ref{tab:auc}) show the two outer bins are the strongest single detectors (AUC $0.88$--$0.92$), but several intermediate fractions also discriminate on their own (up to $\sim$0.85, varying by zone), and even the four most poorly identified bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.5\;\umsmm$, posterior CV $> 0.7$) reach AUC $0.82$ (PZ) / $0.81$ (TZ) when used together. The weakest single bins are zone-dependent: $D = 20\;\umsmm$ is near chance in both zones (AUC $0.52$/$0.55$), while $D = 1.5$ and $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ trade places---$D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ is near chance in PZ ($0.59$) but discriminates in TZ ($0.79$), and $D = 1.5\;\umsmm$ the reverse (PZ $0.83$, TZ $0.55$)---these being the collinear intermediate bins whose weight is poorly resolved within each ROI's posterior (Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}). The spectrum is therefore not split into informative and uninformative bins: the inner fractions co-vary with the dominant tumor axis, so each is individually informative yet contributes nothing once the two outer compartments are present.
Detection AUCs varied by $< 0.03$ across $C = 0.1$--$10$. Grade-classification AUCs are not reported---the $n = 29$ graded tumors are too few for a reliable estimate---and grade-dependent spectral changes are taken up in the Discussion.

\subsection{ADC equals the spectral discriminant}

Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant---the trained logistic-regression score applied to each ROI's spectrum---in both zones and for both spectral estimators (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}). Ranking ROIs by ADC is therefore almost identical to ranking them by the optimal spectral classifier: using NUTS posterior-mean features at $C = 1.0$, the Spearman rank correlation was $\rho = -0.979$ (PZ, $n = 81$) and $\rho = -0.977$ (TZ, $n = 68$), with Pearson correlations of $-0.977$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.985,\,-0.964]$) and $-0.981$ ($[-0.988,\,-0.971]$). Constrained-MAP features gave equivalent values (Spearman PZ $-0.980$, TZ $-0.929$; Pearson $-0.972$, $-0.967$). The near-identity was robust across regularization strengths ($C = 0.1$ to $10$) and held for both MAP and NUTS, indicating that it is a property of the data rather than of any particular classifier setting.
