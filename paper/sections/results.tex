\section{Results}

\subsection{Diffusivity spectra and identifiability}

The trace-decay signal-to-noise ratio ($S_0/\sigma$ from the per-ROI NUTS posterior) had a cohort median of 303 (interquartile range 176--478; full range 25--1548), the spread reflecting tissue composition and distance from the endorectal coil.
At this SNR the eight components are constrained very unequally.
The Fisher correlation matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a) shows the low- and intermediate-diffusivity components ($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) mutually correlated above $0.99$, and the single-component decay curves (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}c) show that $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ contributes signal across all 15 b-values whereas components with $D \geq 3.0\;\umsmm$ fall below the noise floor within the first few steps.

The per-ROI posterior coefficient of variation (CV; per-ROI values in the supplementary atlas, Figure~\ref{fig:atlas}) follows this structure.
Restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$), free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$), and the perfusion proxy ($D = 20.0\;\umsmm$) were well constrained (cohort-mean CV $0.20$, $0.32$, $0.36$); $D = 1.5$ and $2.0\;\umsmm$ were intermediate ($0.74$, $0.52$); and $D = 0.50$, $0.75$, and $1.00\;\umsmm$ were poorly constrained ($0.81$--$0.83$), these three carrying the smallest fractions so that their posterior standard deviation approached their mean.
Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b places the achievable precision on an absolute scale at the cohort-median SNR and shows it recovered in two steps: the unconstrained CRLB is large---for the intermediate bins it exceeds the $[0,1]$ range a fraction can occupy---approximating the half-normal prior by a Gaussian (the van Trees bound) tightens the variance by up to two orders of magnitude, and enforcing non-negativity (the NUTS posterior, under the true half-normal prior) tightens it by a further $2$--$74\times$, together rendering a classically ill-posed problem tractable.

Figure~\ref{fig:spectra} shows the resulting cohort-averaged MAP and NUTS spectra by tissue type and zone.
Relative to normal tissue, tumor carried a larger restricted-diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$: PZ $16.6\%$ vs.\ $5.7\%$, TZ $25.0\%$ vs.\ $8.5\%$; NUTS) and a smaller free-water fraction ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$: PZ $21.0\%$ vs.\ $47.5\%$, TZ $14.5\%$ vs.\ $29.9\%$), with MAP and NUTS in close agreement.

\subsection{Tumor detection}

Table~\ref{tab:auc} and Figure~\ref{fig:roc} summarize detection performance.
ADC was the strongest single detector (raw-rank AUC PZ $0.951$, TZ $0.979$), and the spectral classifiers matched it: through the identical leave-one-out pipeline the eight-fraction logistic regression reached PZ $0.926$ / TZ $0.923$ (NUTS) and PZ $0.917$ / TZ $0.952$ (MAP), with bootstrap confidence intervals overlapping ADC's throughout.
The fair head-to-head is ADC versus the spectral models scored through the same held-out pipeline; the in-sample ADC raw rank is reported as the hardest reference baseline.
Restricting the classifier to the two outer fractions $\{D = 0.25,\,3.0\;\umsmm\}$ did not reduce performance (NUTS PZ $0.933$, TZ $0.937$; MAP TZ $0.965$). The mirror ablation pins down where the signal lives: classifying on the six inner fractions alone---the two outer compartments removed---dropped NUTS detection to PZ $0.817$ / TZ $0.775$ (Table~\ref{tab:auc}). Detection is thus both carried and bounded by the two outer compartments; the inner fractions add only finite-sample noise once those are present, which is why the two-feature model matches, and sometimes slightly exceeds, the eight-feature one.
This reflects redundancy rather than uselessness. The individual-fraction AUCs (Table~\ref{tab:auc}) show the two outer bins are the strongest single detectors (AUC $0.88$--$0.92$), but several intermediate fractions also discriminate on their own (up to $\sim$0.85, varying by zone), and even the four most poorly identified bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.5\;\umsmm$, posterior CV $> 0.7$) reach AUC $0.82$ (PZ) / $0.81$ (TZ) when used together. The spectrum is therefore not split into informative and uninformative bins: the inner fractions co-vary with the dominant tumor axis, so each is individually informative yet contributes nothing once the two outer compartments are present.
Grade-classification AUCs are not reported ($n = 29$ graded tumors are too imprecise; see Table~\ref{tab:auc}); grading is instead presented below as grade-dependent spectral shifts.
Detection AUCs varied by $< 0.03$ across $C = 0.1$--$10$.

\subsection{Why ADC works}

Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant---the trained logistic-regression score applied to each ROI's spectrum---in both zones and for both spectral estimators (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}). Ranking ROIs by ADC is therefore almost identical to ranking them by the optimal spectral classifier: using NUTS posterior-mean features at $C = 1.0$, the Spearman rank correlation was $\rho = -0.979$ (PZ, $n = 81$) and $\rho = -0.977$ (TZ, $n = 68$), with Pearson correlations of $-0.977$ ($95\%$ bootstrap CI $[-0.985,\,-0.964]$) and $-0.981$ ($[-0.988,\,-0.971]$). Constrained-MAP features gave equivalent values (Spearman PZ $-0.980$, TZ $-0.929$; Pearson $-0.972$, $-0.967$). The near-identity was robust across regularization strengths ($C = 0.1$ to $10$) and held for both MAP and NUTS, indicating that it is a property of the data rather than of any particular classifier setting.

The mechanism is visible in the per-bin structure of the discriminant (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).
The ADC sensitivity vector $\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ at the average tumor spectrum is strongly negative for restricted diffusion ($-1.59$: a larger restricted fraction lowers ADC) and positive for free water ($+0.85$); because the logistic-regression coefficients point the opposite way, Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity} plots the sign-flipped $-\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ alongside them, so aligned bars indicate matching direction.
Using NUTS features, the two vectors align well (Pearson $r = -0.79$, PZ, $95\%$ CI $[-0.89,\,-0.50]$; $r = -0.88$, TZ, $[-0.95,\,-0.70]$), and the alignment is carried by the two outer bins: only $D = 0.25$ and $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ have logistic coefficients whose bootstrap intervals exclude zero.
This is not because ADC ignores the intermediate compartments: its sensitivity $-\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ is appreciable there (e.g.\ $D = 0.5\;\umsmm$), since ADC is a smooth, monotonic-in-$D$ weighting of the entire spectrum. The resolution is that discriminative power is sensitivity \emph{times} tumor--normal contrast: the intermediate fractions carry little contrast beyond what the outer bins already capture (Table~\ref{tab:auc}) and are poorly identified (high CV), so ADC's sensitivity to them is spent on a low-contrast, noisy direction that shifts neither score's ranking. Both summaries therefore reduce to the same two outer compartments, which is why the per-ROI scores rank almost identically (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}) even though the per-bin profiles diverge in the middle. The divergence is confined to bins that are diagnostically inert for both---it is not evidence that ADC and the spectral classifier estimate different quantities.
\subsection{Spectral Decomposition by Gleason Grade Group}

Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg} shows the cohort's average NUTS posterior-mean spectrum by Gleason Grade Group, with normal tissue ($n = 109$) as the shared baseline and tumor resolved into GGG\,$=\,1$ ($n = 8$), GGG\,$=\,2$ ($n = 12$), and GGG\,$\geq 3$ ($n = 9$).

The free-diffusion bin at $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ (intact gland lumen) is suppressed as soon as tumor is present ($0.39$ normal $\to 0.21$ at GGG\,$=\,1$) and then changes little ($0.16$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$): it saturates at tumor onset, separating tumor from normal but not grade from grade.
By contrast the restricted-cellular bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises with grade ($0.07$ normal to $0.31$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$) and the glandular-epithelial bin ($D = 2.0\;\umsmm$) is preserved in low-grade tumor then depleted ($0.26$ normal, $0.28$ at GGG\,$=\,1$, $0.17$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$): these bins, where ADC is least sensitive (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}), track aggressiveness.
Grade-specific sample sizes are small (down to $n = 8$--$9$) and the corresponding confidence bands wide, and 11 of the 40 tumor ROIs (7 with biopsy Gleason Grade Group 0 and 4 ungraded) lack a Grade Group and are excluded from this grade stratification, though they remain tumor ROIs in the detection analysis.
Method robustness: re-running the same group averaging on tuned-MAP ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$) posterior estimates rather than NUTS posterior means yielded indistinguishable group trajectories on all eight bins.

\subsection{Prediction confidence}

Each classifier returns a probability $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ rather than a hard label, and misclassified ROIs concentrate near the decision boundary ($P \approx 0.5$): ranking predictions by their distance from the boundary separates likely errors from confident, correct calls, so the classifier flags its own least-reliable predictions (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}).
This confidence is a property of the logistic model, available from the point estimate alone, and is shared by the ADC- and spectrum-based classifiers.
Propagating the full NUTS posterior through the classifier---applying it to every posterior draw rather than the posterior mean---does not add to this.
The 90\% credible interval on $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ is on average $2.4\times$ wider for misclassified than for correctly classified ROIs (pooled across zones, mean width $0.39$ vs $0.16$), but this reflects the logistic geometry rather than the spectral posterior: the slope $P(1-P)$ is largest at the boundary, so in logit space the difference falls to $\approx 1.3\times$ and is no longer significant, and the interval width does not predict misclassification once distance to the boundary is accounted for.
Offering the per-component posterior standard deviations to the classifier as features likewise leaves detection AUC essentially unchanged.
The posterior therefore quantifies which spectral components are reliably estimated (identifiability), but adds no diagnostic information to the tumor-versus-normal decision beyond the point estimate.

\subsection{Pixel-wise Spectral Decomposition}

Pixel-wise spectral estimation was performed on the manually segmented prostate of the supplementary patient (MAP and NUTS; all NUTS voxels converged, $\hat{R} < 1.05$).
At the voxel level the restricted-diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) fraction maps were spatially anti-correlated (Figure~\ref{fig:pixelwise}), and the per-voxel spectral discriminant (ROI-level PZ coefficients applied to the pixel spectra) reproduced the ADC pattern (anti-correlation $r = -0.97$).
The contribution unique to the Bayesian decomposition is the accompanying per-voxel uncertainty map: propagating the NUTS posterior through the classifier flags where a voxel's score is least reliable, and the eight-bin detector is on average $1.8\times$ more uncertain than the two-bin detector because it weights the unidentifiable intermediate compartments---an uncertainty that ADC and point-estimate methods cannot provide.
As at the ROI level, the discriminant adds no detection signal beyond ADC; its value here is the calibrated, spatially-resolved confidence it carries.
Direction independence was verified at the whole-ROI level on a separate three-direction acquisition (Figure~\ref{fig:directions}): across all 13 regions the per-direction coefficient of variation of the estimated spectrum was lowest for the diagnostically dominant restricted-diffusion bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, mean $9\%$) and was large only on the near-empty bins ($D = 20\;\umsmm$), where it reflects a small-denominator effect rather than genuine anisotropy, confirming that trace averaging is appropriate for spectral decomposition.
