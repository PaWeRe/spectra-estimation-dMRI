\section*{Appendix: Cram\'er-Rao Scaling of Bin Spacing}

Consider a simplified two-component model in which the normalized signal at
b-value $b$ is
%
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{S(b)}{S_0} = p\,e^{-bD} + (1-p)\,e^{-b(D+\Delta D)},
\end{equation*}
%
with additive Gaussian noise of standard deviation $\sigma$.
The Fisher information for the fraction parameter $p$ is
%
\begin{equation*}
  I(p) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\left(e^{-b_i D} - e^{-b_i(D+\Delta D)}\right)^2.
\end{equation*}
%
Approximating the sum by an integral over $[0,\,b_{\max}]$, expanding the
square, and evaluating in the regime $b_{\max} D \gg 1$ so that the
exponential boundary terms vanish:
%
\begin{equation*}
  I(p) \propto \frac{1}{2D} - \frac{2}{2D + \Delta D} + \frac{1}{2(D + \Delta D)}.
\end{equation*}
%
For $\Delta D \ll D$, a Taylor expansion yields
$I(p) \propto (\Delta D)^2 / (4D^3)$.
Setting $I = \text{const}$ to attain equal estimation precision for all bin pairs,
gives $\Delta D \propto D^{3/2}$, Equation~\ref{eq:spacing_scaling} in the
main text.
The same scaling is obtained from the full two-parameter model ($p$ and
$q = 1-p$ independent) via inversion of the $2 \times 2$ Fisher matrix.
This argument is intentionally restricted to the two-compartment limit; the
full multi-compartment problem is treated numerically in the main text.

\section*{Supporting Information Figure Legends}

\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:atlas}.}
Representative per-ROI NUTS posterior spectra, for different anatomical zones and Gleason Grade Groups. Five patients are shown (rows), each
contributing a same-patient normal (left column) and tumor (right column) ROI
from a single zone, so the within-patient spectral shift can be read directly;
they span Grade Group 1, 3, and 5 in the peripheral zone and Grade Group 1 and 5
in the transition zone. Each panel shows the full NUTS posterior over the eight-bin
diffusivity
spectrum ($D = 0.25$--$20\;\umsmm$, left to right) drawn as box plots: box $=$
interquartile range, solid line $=$ posterior median, dashed line $=$ posterior
mean, whiskers $=$ 5th--95th percentiles (outliers suppressed; the per-bin
posteriors are continuous and unimodal). Each box face is shaded by the
within-ROI per-bin coefficient of variation (CV $=$ posterior std $/$ mean) in four bands---$< 0.4$, $0.4$--$0.6$, $0.6$--$0.8$, $> 0.8$---from light (well identified) to
dark (poorly identified); the box
\emph{width} is the absolute posterior spread and the color the \emph{relative}
spread, so a low-mass bin can show a narrow box yet a dark color. The
green $\times$ represents the tuned-MAP ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$) estimate. The
restricted-diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises and the free-water
fraction ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) falls with grade, while the intermediate bins
remain the least identifiable.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}.}
NUTS sampler convergence for five representative ROIs: three tumors
spanning the cohort SNR range (low, intermediate, high) and one normal
peripheral-zone and one normal transition-zone ROI, so convergence behavior can
be compared across both SNR and tissue type. For each ROI the left panel traces
a single chain (first 300 of 2000 post-warmup draws, shown for legibility) of the
eight normalized spectral fractions (colors): the well-determined restricted bin
($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and the two largest-spread intermediate bins are highlighted,
each shown as the faded raw draws overlaid with a running mean (window of 11 draws shown in bold) that suppresses the uncorrelated high-frequency noise and exposes the
slower anti-correlated weight-swapping; the remaining bins are faint. The right
panel shows the corresponding per-fraction autocorrelation (all eight bins, averaged
over the four chains) versus sample lag. The restricted-diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) is
tightly pinned (posterior std $\approx 0.02$ at every SNR), whereas the more collinear
intermediate fractions ($D = 1.5$--$3.0\;\umsmm$) explore a wider range and
trade weight against one another (pairwise posterior correlations $\approx -0.5$
to $-0.7$): the data constrain the total intermediate mass far better than its
distribution across these bins. This is the sampling signature of the design
collinearity (see the near-$\pm 1$ Fisher correlations of
Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}), not a failure to converge --- the
worst per-ROI split-$\hat{R}$ (annotated, all $\leq 1.01$) and the rapid
autocorrelation decay (within $\sim$10 lags) confirm efficient mixing; the
dispersion broadens as SNR falls.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:directions}.}
Direction independence of whole-ROI spectral estimates validates trace
averaging.
Per-encoding-direction MAP spectra (three encoding directions, colored) versus
the trace-averaged spectrum (black) for four representative patients (rows),
each with one normal (left) and one tumor (right) region of interest; the
anatomical zone (peripheral or transition) is labeled per panel.
The three directions agree closely in the diagnostically dominant
restricted-diffusion bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$; mean direction-wise CV $9\%$
across all 13 evaluated directional regions); the larger inter-direction spread in the
intermediate and near-empty bins ($D = 0.5$--$2.0$ and $D = 20\;\umsmm$)
mirrors their poor identifiability rather than reflecting genuine anisotropy,
with the per-direction CV tracking each bin's inverse mass.
Because tumor detection rests on the well-identified restricted and free-water compartments, trace
averaging is well justified; aggregate per-bin statistics are reported in
Results.
MAP estimates are shown (constrained ridge, $\lambda = 10^{-3}$); these
directional ROIs are from a separate patient cohort acquired earlier, outside the 149-ROI cohort.
