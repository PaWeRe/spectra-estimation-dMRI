\section*{Appendix: Cram\'er-Rao Scaling of Bin Spacing}

Consider a simplified two-component model in which the normalized signal at
b-value $b$ is
%
\begin{equation*}
  \frac{S(b)}{S_0} = p\,e^{-bD} + (1-p)\,e^{-b(D+\Delta D)},
\end{equation*}
%
with additive Gaussian noise of standard deviation $\sigma$.
The Fisher information for the fraction parameter $p$ is
%
\begin{equation*}
  I(p) = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\left(e^{-b_i D} - e^{-b_i(D+\Delta D)}\right)^2.
\end{equation*}
%
Approximating the sum by an integral over $[0,\,b_{\max}]$, expanding the
square, and evaluating in the regime $b_{\max} D \gg 1$ (so that the
exponential boundary terms vanish):
%
\begin{equation*}
  I(p) \propto \frac{1}{2D} - \frac{2}{2D + \Delta D} + \frac{1}{2(D + \Delta D)}.
\end{equation*}
%
For $\Delta D \ll D$, a Taylor expansion yields
$I(p) \propto (\Delta D)^2 / (4D^3)$.
Setting $I = \text{const}$ (equal estimation precision for all bin pairs)
gives $\Delta D \propto D^{3/2}$, Equation~\ref{eq:spacing_scaling} in the
main text.
The same scaling is obtained from the full two-parameter model ($p$ and
$q = 1-p$ independent) via inversion of the $2 \times 2$ Fisher matrix.
This argument is intentionally restricted to the two-compartment limit; the
full multi-compartment problem is treated numerically in the main text.

\section*{Supporting Information Figure Legends}

\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:directions}.}
Direction independence of whole-ROI spectral estimates validates trace
averaging.
Per-encoding-direction MAP spectra (three encoding directions, colored) versus
the trace-averaged spectrum (black) for four representative patients (rows),
each with one normal (left) and one tumor (right) region of interest; the
anatomical zone (peripheral or transition) is labeled per panel.
The three directions agree closely in the diagnostically dominant
restricted-diffusion bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$; mean direction-wise CV $9\%$
across all 13 directional regions); the larger inter-direction spread in the
intermediate and near-empty bins ($D = 0.5$--$2.0$ and $D = 20\;\umsmm$)
mirrors their poor identifiability rather than reflecting genuine anisotropy,
the per-direction CV tracking each bin's inverse mass.
Because tumor detection rests on the well-identified outer compartments, trace
averaging is well justified; aggregate per-bin statistics are reported in
Results.
MAP estimates are shown (constrained ridge, $\lambda = 10^{-3}$); these
directional ROIs lie outside the 149-ROI cohort, and MAP and NUTS are
equivalent on realistic spectra (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}).

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:atlas}.}
Representative per-ROI NUTS posterior spectra across anatomical zone and Gleason
Grade Group. Eight ROIs are shown (rows: peripheral zone, transition zone;
columns: normal tissue and tumor at Grade Group 1, 3, and 5), each the full NUTS
posterior over the eight-bin diffusivity spectrum
($D = 0.25$--$20\;\umsmm$, left to right) drawn as box plots: box $=$
interquartile range, solid line $=$ posterior median, dashed line $=$ posterior
mean, whiskers $=$ 5th--95th percentiles (fliers suppressed; the per-bin
posteriors are continuous and unimodal). Each box face is coloured by the
within-ROI per-bin coefficient of variation (CV $=$ posterior std $/$ mean;
colorblind-safe sequential colour plus hatching, light $=$ well identified to
dark $=$ poorly identified, the scheme of Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}); the box
\emph{width} is the absolute posterior spread and the colour the \emph{relative}
spread, so a low-mass outer bin can show a narrow box yet a dark colour. The
green $\times$ is the tuned-MAP ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$) estimate. The
restricted-diffusion fraction ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises and the free-water
fraction ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) falls with grade, while the intermediate bins
remain the least identifiable. Panel titles give the (anonymized) patient
identifier, zone, tissue, Grade Group, and SNR; a companion key file
(\texttt{figS\_subset\_key.csv}) lists the exact anatomical region per panel.
This representative subset stands in for a full per-ROI atlas; the complete
149-ROI inference is reproducible from the released pipeline.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Figure~\ref{fig:convergence}.}
NUTS sampler convergence for the same eight representative ROIs (one page per
zone). For each ROI the left panel is the trace of all eight normalized spectral
fractions (colours) across the four concatenated chains (2000 post-warmup draws
each; dotted lines mark chain boundaries), and the right panel the corresponding
per-fraction autocorrelation (averaged over chains) versus lag. The
well-identified outer fractions ($D = 0.25$, $3.0$, $20\;\umsmm$) are pinned and
decorrelate within a few tens of draws, whereas the collinear intermediate
fractions explore a wider range yet still mix efficiently; the worst per-ROI
split-$\hat{R}$ (annotated, all $\leq 1.01$) and the rapid autocorrelation decay
confirm convergence. The dispersion of the intermediate fractions is the
sampling signature of their poor identifiability (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}),
not a failure to converge.
