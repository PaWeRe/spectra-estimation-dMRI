\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is the de facto biomarker of prostate diffusion MRI, and compartment-volume models explain why it works but are not measured directly. We use Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate DWI to quantify which diffusion compartments are reliably recoverable and to explain ADC's near-optimality for tumor detection at the region-of-interest level.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b DWI ($b = 0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$, 15 b-values) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components using both maximum a~posteriori (MAP, constrained ridge non-negative least squares) and fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference, the latter providing per-component posterior uncertainty.
Per-component identifiability was summarized by the NUTS posterior coefficient of variation (CV).
Tumor detection in the peripheral and transition zones was evaluated through a single leave-one-out cross-validation pipeline applied identically to ADC, to individual spectral fractions, to the two outer fractions ($D = 0.25$ and $3.0\;\umsmm$), and to all eight fractions, using $L_2$-regularized logistic regression with 95\% confidence intervals from a patient-level bootstrap.
Associations with Gleason grade were assessed as grade-dependent spectral shifts rather than by classification, given the small number of graded tumors ($n = 29$).
A pixel-wise demonstration was performed on a separate patient dataset.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
Only restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, mean posterior CV = 0.20) and free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, mean CV = 0.32) were well identified; the intermediate components ($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) had CV $> 0.80$, a data limit robust to the prior width.
For tumor detection the spectral classifier was statistically indistinguishable from ADC in both zones, and a minimal two-fraction classifier using only the restricted ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) compartments was as accurate as the full eight-fraction model (two-fraction NUTS AUC PZ $0.93$ [$0.86$, $0.98$], TZ $0.94$ [$0.85$, $0.99$]; ADC raw rank PZ $0.95$, TZ $0.98$): tumor-detection signal collapses onto the two outer compartments.
Consistently, ADC scores anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant at $r \approx -0.98$ (95\% bootstrap CI within $[-0.99,\,-0.96]$ in both zones, for both MAP and NUTS features), so ADC rank-orders ROIs almost identically to the optimal spectral classifier.
Grade-dependent spectral shifts dissociated a detection axis (the free-water fraction collapsing at tumor onset) from a grading axis (the restricted and glandular fractions tracking grade), in the intermediate bins where ADC is least sensitive.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion:}
At the ROI level the diffusivity spectrum is diagnostically equivalent to ADC, and the collapse of tumor-detection signal onto two outer compartments---which ADC implicitly averages---explains why a single scalar is so hard to improve upon.
Bayesian spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC but mechanistically explains it, while additionally quantifying which compartments are reliably recoverable and providing per-component posterior uncertainty; tuned MAP and NUTS yield equivalent spectra on realistic tissue, so the Bayesian gain is calibrated uncertainty rather than better point estimates.

\end{abstract}
