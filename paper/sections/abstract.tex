\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is the de facto biomarker of prostate diffusion MRI, yet the multi-compartment models that would explain its success are not measured directly. Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate diffusion-weighted imaging was used to determine which diffusion compartments are reliably recoverable and to explain ADC's near-optimality for region-of-interest (ROI) tumor detection.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b diffusion-weighted imaging (15 b-values up to 3500~s/mm$^2$) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components by constrained-ridge maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation and by fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference, the latter also inferring per-ROI noise and per-component uncertainty. Tumor detection was assessed by leave-one-out logistic regression applied identically to ADC, individual fractions, the two outer fractions (@Stephan: The two outer fractions would be 0.25 and 20. May be rather refer to as "the slowest diffusion fraction and the (luminal) free water diffusion fraction".), and all eight, with patient-level bootstrap confidence intervals.
