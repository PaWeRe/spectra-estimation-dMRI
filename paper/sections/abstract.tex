\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is the de facto biomarker of prostate diffusion MRI, yet the multi-compartment models that would explain its success are not measured directly. Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate diffusion-weighted imaging was used to determine which diffusion compartments are reliably recoverable and to explain ADC's near-optimality for region-of-interest (ROI) tumor detection.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b diffusion-weighted imaging (15 b-values up to 3500~s/mm$^2$) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components by constrained-ridge maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimation and by fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference, the latter also inferring per-ROI noise and per-component uncertainty. Tumor detection was assessed by leave-one-out logistic regression applied identically to ADC, individual fractions, the two outer fractions, and all eight, with patient-level bootstrap confidence intervals.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
Only restricted diffusion and free water were well identified; the intermediate components were largely undetermined by the data, a limit robust to prior width. The spectral classifier was statistically indistinguishable from ADC, and a two-fraction model matched the full eight, the detection signal concentrating on the two outer compartments. Across ROIs, ADC was near-perfectly anti-correlated with the optimal spectral discriminant for both estimators. MAP and NUTS yielded equivalent spectra and equivalent classification, and propagating the posterior added no diagnostic information beyond the point estimate.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion:}
At the ROI level the diffusivity spectrum is diagnostically equivalent to ADC, which it mechanistically explains rather than supersedes. The Bayesian treatment's contribution is to quantify which compartments an acquisition can resolve---here, restricted diffusion and free water alone.

\end{abstract}
