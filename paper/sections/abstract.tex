\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is the de facto biomarker of prostate diffusion MRI and is hard to outperform for detection despite richer multi-compartment models. Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate DWI was used to quantify which diffusion compartments are recoverable and to explain ADC's near-optimality for region-of-interest (ROI) tumor detection.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b DWI ($b = 0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$, 15 values) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components by constrained-ridge maximum \emph{a posteriori} (MAP) estimation and by fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference, which jointly infers the spectrum and noise level and yields per-component posterior uncertainty. Detection was evaluated by leave-one-out logistic regression applied identically to ADC and to spectral classifiers (individual, two-outer, and eight-fraction), with patient-level bootstrap confidence intervals. Identifiability was assessed by Fisher information and the posterior, grade associations as grade-dependent spectral shifts ($n = 29$).

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
Only restricted diffusion and free water were well identified; intermediate components were poorly resolved, a data limit robust to prior width. Detection collapsed onto the two outer compartments: a two-fraction classifier matched the full eight (AUC 0.93--0.94) and was indistinguishable from ADC (raw rank 0.95--0.98), and ADC anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant at $|r| > 0.95$ for both estimators. Grade-dependent shifts appeared in the less-identifiable intermediate compartments, distinct from detection.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion:}
At the ROI level the diffusivity spectrum is diagnostically equivalent to ADC, explaining why it is so hard to improve upon for detection. Bayesian decomposition does not replace ADC but mechanistically explains it while quantifying compartment recoverability: MAP provides fast point estimates, and the posterior adds per-component and per-prediction uncertainty.

\end{abstract}
