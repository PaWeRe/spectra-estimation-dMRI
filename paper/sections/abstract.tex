\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
To investigate, both theoretically and on patient data, how reliably each component of the diffusivity spectrum can be recovered from extended b-value prostate diffusion-weighted MRI, and to characterize the relationship between the conventional apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) and the optimal multi-component spectral classifier for tumor detection.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b DWI ($b = 0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$, 15 b-values) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components using both maximum a~posteriori (MAP, ridge non-negative least squares) and fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference.
Per-component identifiability was assessed both theoretically through the Cram\'er-Rao lower bound on the Fisher information matrix and practically through the NUTS posterior coefficient of variation.
Tumor detection in the peripheral and transition zones was evaluated through a single leave-one-out cross-validation pipeline applied identically to ADC, to individual spectral fractions, to the two outer fractions ($D = 0.25$ and $3.0\;\umsmm$), and to all eight fractions, using $L_2$-regularized logistic regression, with 95\% confidence intervals from a patient-level bootstrap.
Associations with Gleason grade were assessed as grade-dependent spectral shifts and rank correlations rather than by classification, given the small number of graded tumors ($n = 29$).
A pixel-wise demonstration was performed on a separate patient dataset.

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
Only restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, mean posterior CV = 0.20) and free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, mean CV = 0.32) were well identified; intermediate components ($D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$) had CV $> 0.80$, consistent with mutual Fisher correlation $> 0.99$ at the SNR realized in this cohort (median 303).
Non-negativity constraints and Bayesian priors reduced effective estimation error by 100--8000$\times$ relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound.
For tumor detection the spectral classifier was statistically indistinguishable from ADC in both zones, and a minimal two-fraction classifier using only the restricted ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) and free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) compartments was as accurate as the full eight-fraction model (two-fraction NUTS AUC PZ $0.93$ [$0.86$, $0.98$], TZ $0.94$ [$0.85$, $0.99$]; ADC raw rank PZ $0.95$, TZ $0.98$); detection signal was concentrated in these two outer compartments.
Across patients, ADC scores anti-correlated with the optimal spectral discriminant at $r \approx -0.98$ (95\% bootstrap CI within $[-0.99,\,-0.97]$ in both zones), and the ADC sensitivity vector aligned with the learned classification direction at $|r| > 0.93$, providing a mechanistic explanation for ADC's near-optimality.

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion:}
ADC is a near-optimal linear projection of the prostate diffusivity spectrum for tumor detection.
Bayesian spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC but explains it, while additionally quantifying which spectral compartments are reliably recoverable, identifying a systematic MAP underestimation of the acinar-lumen fraction, and providing per-voxel uncertainty for pixel-wise tissue characterization.

\end{abstract}
