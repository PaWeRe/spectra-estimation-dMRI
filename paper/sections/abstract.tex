\begin{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Purpose:}
The apparent diffusion coefficient (ADC) is the de facto biomarker of prostate diffusion MRI, yet the compartment-volume models that explain its success are not measured directly. We use Bayesian spectral decomposition of extended b-value prostate DWI to quantify which diffusion compartments are reliably recoverable and to explain ADC's near-optimality for region-of-interest (ROI) tumor detection.

\noindent\textbf{Methods:}
Multi-b DWI ($b = 0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$, 15 values) from 56 patients (149 ROIs: 40 tumor, 109 normal) was decomposed into eight diffusivity components by constrained-ridge MAP and fully Bayesian No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) inference, the latter providing per-component posterior uncertainty (coefficient of variation, CV). Tumor detection was evaluated through one leave-one-out logistic-regression pipeline applied identically to ADC, individual fractions, the two outer fractions ($D = 0.25$ and $3.0\;\umsmm$), and all eight, with patient-level bootstrap confidence intervals. Gleason associations were assessed as grade-dependent spectral shifts ($n = 29$ graded).

\noindent\textbf{Results:}
Only restricted diffusion (CV = 0.20) and free water (CV = 0.32) were well identified; intermediate components had CV $> 0.80$, a data limit robust to prior width. The spectral classifier was statistically indistinguishable from ADC, and a two-fraction model matched the full eight (NUTS AUC 0.93--0.94; ADC raw rank 0.95--0.98): detection signal collapses onto the two outer compartments. ADC anti-correlated with the spectral discriminant at $|r| > 0.95$ for both estimators. Grade-dependent shifts dissociated a detection axis (free water collapsing at tumor onset) from a grading axis (restricted and glandular fractions tracking grade).

\noindent\textbf{Conclusion:}
At the ROI level the diffusivity spectrum is diagnostically equivalent to ADC, which explains why this scalar is so hard to improve upon. Bayesian decomposition does not obsolete ADC but mechanistically explains it while quantifying compartment recoverability and per-component uncertainty; tuned MAP and NUTS yield equivalent spectra, so the Bayesian gain is calibrated uncertainty, not better point estimates.

\end{abstract}
