\section{Discussion}

The central finding of this study is that ADC, despite reducing multi-component diffusion information to a single scalar, achieves near-optimal spectral discrimination for prostate tumor detection.
The anti-correlation between ADC scores and the optimal spectral discriminant ($r \approx -0.98$ across zones and inference methods) shows that ranking patients by ADC is, to close approximation, identical to ranking them by the optimal eight-component classifier: ADC is already a near-optimal linear combination of the diffusivity spectrum for distinguishing tumor from normal tissue.
The mechanism is visible per bin (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}): the discriminant's stable weight and ADC's sensitivity both concentrate on the two outer compartments, so the two are aligned on the detection axis---moderately at the bin level (the near-unity alignment seen under heavy regularization is a smoothing artifact), but near-perfectly at the ROI-score level.

Rather than an impoverished summary of a richer signal, ADC effectively captures the dominant axis of spectral variation relevant to tumor detection.
The spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC; it explains it.
The added value of multi-component analysis lies in three areas: biological interpretability (which compartments---restricted diffusion, free water---drive the ADC contrast); uncertainty quantification (which components are reliably estimated); and the potential to detect signatures orthogonal to the tumor--normal axis, such as glandular-water differences between Gleason grades.
Our ADC follows PI-RADS and includes $b = 0$, which renders it sensitive to the vascular ($D = 20\;\umsmm$) compartment via the intercept term; excluding the perfusion-dominated low-b image can shift discrimination performance either way depending on cohort and protocol \citep{aksitciris2019accelerated} and could be examined in future work.

A candidate histological interpretation of the grade-dependent spectral shifts (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}) follows from the cellular reorganization characteristic of prostate adenocarcinoma.
The dominant free-diffusion bin at $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$---bulk water in gland lacunae of $\approx$100--300\,\textmu m---collapses in both tumor groups, consistent with replacement of patent lumina by solid tissue.
The $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ bin is more grade-specific: low-grade GGG\,$=\,1$ lesions retain near-normal glandular architecture and $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ mass, whereas the loss of glandular structure at higher Gleason patterns collapses this compartment too.
The redistribution of displaced spectral mass toward $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, most pronounced in GGG\,$\geq 3$, is consistent with the increased cell-packing density that is the morphological hallmark of higher-grade cancer, and appears to track the predominant-Gleason-pattern transition at which ADC-based grading is itself least reliable---although the small per-grade samples preclude a statistical claim.
Any incremental grading signal beyond ADC would have to come from the intermediate bins, where ADC is least sensitive (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}); but their wide posterior uncertainty (CV~$> 0.8$) and the small grade-specific samples preclude a statistical claim.
We present this histology mapping as a candidate interpretation the decomposition makes directly observable, not a validated finding; testing it would require a larger cohort with whole-mount histology correlation.

The comparison between MAP and NUTS estimation revealed a complementary relationship.
At tuned regularisation ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$), MAP and NUTS agreed closely for the well-identified components and their discriminant scores were nearly interchangeable ($r > 0.98$), confirming that closed-form MAP is sufficient for classification and recovers approximately the same fractions as the full sampler on realistic spectra.
The divergence reported at $\lambda = 0.1$ (MAP underestimating the $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ fraction by $\approx$35\,\%) is therefore a regularisation artifact, not an intrinsic property of point estimation: at small $\lambda$ the ridge penalty no longer smooths dominant bins into their neighbors, and the constrained program (Equation~\ref{eq:map}) recovers the lumen fraction.
The divergence between MAP and NUTS on intermediate components ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) was not a deficiency of either method but a faithful reflection of the ill-posedness: these components are genuinely undetermined by 15 b-value measurements, even at the much lower noise levels achieved with the endorectal coil and low-resolution acquisition of the present cohort.
NUTS makes this ill-posedness explicit through wide posterior intervals (CV $> 0.80$), reflecting the same Bayesian gain (effective estimation-error reduction relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound) that makes the well-identified components quantitatively trustworthy; MAP, in contrast, silently returns a point estimate that may be misleadingly precise.
The Bayesian approach thus identifies what can and cannot be learned from a given acquisition \citep{prange2009quantifying}.
The posterior can also be propagated through the classifier itself: applying the trained logistic regression to every NUTS draw of an ROI's spectrum, rather than to its posterior mean, converts each prediction into a distribution over $P(\mathrm{tumor})$---a calibrated probability with a credible interval rather than a label (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}).
This propagated uncertainty is diagnostically meaningful: the 90\% credible interval on $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ is on average $2.4\times$ wider for misclassified than for correctly classified ROIs (mean $0.39$ vs $0.16$; $p = 0.003$), so posterior uncertainty acts as an intrinsic, model-internal flag for low-confidence predictions.
This effect is partly geometric: the credible interval also widens toward the decision boundary, but the logistic link's slope $P(1-P)$ is maximal at $P = 0.5$, so even constant uncertainty in the linear predictor would widen probability intervals there.
Measured in logit space, which removes this geometry, the spread is still $\approx 1.3\times$ larger for misclassified ROIs, confirming a genuine component, amplified in probability space because errors concentrate near the boundary.
The few ROIs confidently misclassified with narrow intervals are almost all low-grade (GGG\,$=\,1$) or ungraded tumors whose spectra are genuinely indistinguishable from normal tissue (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}): the posterior is correctly confident about a spectrum that carries no detectable tumor signal---a limit of diffusion contrast for low-grade disease, not a miscalibration.
We emphasise that this propagation is exploratory and illustrates a capability unique to full posterior sampling---a MAP point estimate cannot produce these intervals---rather than a validated diagnostic.

The Fisher information analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}) provides a theoretical foundation for the observed identifiability pattern.
The Fisher correlation matrix reveals that intermediate diffusivity components are mutually correlated above $r = 0.99$, making them fundamentally indistinguishable regardless of the estimation method.
The Cram\'er-Rao analysis yields a design principle---minimum resolvable bin spacing scales as $\Delta D \propto D^{3/2}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:spacing_scaling})---but numerical evaluation of the full $8 \times 8$ Fisher matrix shows that no rearrangement of bins eliminates the ill-conditioning of intermediate components; it merely shifts it along the diffusivity axis.
This confirms that the poor identifiability of the $D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$ range is an intrinsic property of the Laplace inversion at the SNR realized here, not an artifact of suboptimal discretization.

The unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao lower bound predicts estimation errors far larger than what the Bayesian sampler achieves, but this gap decomposes into two distinct mechanisms rather than a single ratio: incorporating the half-normal prior (the van Trees Bayesian CRLB) tightens the unconstrained bound by up to two orders of magnitude, and the non-negativity constraint together with the one-sided prior tightens the realized posterior by a further $2$--$74\times$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b).
This demonstrates that non-negativity constraints and prior information are not merely convenient regularization but are essential for making the spectral decomposition tractable, and it resolves an apparent discrepancy between earlier CRLB-based pessimism about multi-compartment fitting and the practical recoverability we observe.

This study has several limitations.
First, the tumor ROIs were placed based on multiparametric MRI (including DWI/ADC maps), introducing a potential circularity: lesions that are well delineated on ADC maps may be overrepresented \citep{langkilde2018evaluation}.
This bias could inflate ADC's apparent discriminative performance relative to spectral features.
Only the Gleason grade classification, derived from biopsy pathology, is free from this circularity.
Second, the GGG sample size ($n = 29$) is insufficient for reliable AUC comparison between methods; differences below $\Delta\text{AUC} \approx 0.10$ cannot be meaningfully distinguished.
Third, the spectral model assumes a fixed set of diffusivity bins; a continuous spectral approach \citep{prange2009quantifying} might reveal additional structure but at the cost of increased ill-posedness.
Fourth, only one fixed echo-time/diffusion-time configuration was analyzed; the shorter echo and diffusion times enabled by recent gradient hardware \citep{zhu2024human, elsaid2026nonlinear} could change both the SNR and the relative weight of the compartments visible to the decomposition.
Finally, the pixel-wise demonstration (Fig.~\ref{fig:pixelwise}) was limited to a single supplementary patient; generalization requires larger studies with histopathological correlation.
It also exposes a calibration caveat for per-voxel deployment: applying the ROI-trained detector voxel-wise yields scores that skew systematically toward the tumor class, because the substantially lower SNR of a single voxel relative to an averaged ROI inflates the restricted-diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) fraction.
This reinforces our decision to report all quantitative results at the ROI level, and indicates that faithful per-voxel spectral classification would require either explicit voxel-level noise modeling or spatial regularization.

The framework presented here is directly applicable to other organs and tumor types where multi-b DWI is acquired, including breast, liver, and brain tumors.
Amortized inference---training a neural network to approximate the posterior---could reduce computation from minutes per ROI to milliseconds, enabling real-time deployment, and the Fisher information framework could guide joint optimization of b-value protocols and discretization grids for specific clinical questions.
Integration with T\textsubscript{2}-weighted imaging could yield multi-parametric spectral biomarkers.
