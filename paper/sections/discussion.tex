\section{Discussion}
The central finding is that ADC, despite collapsing the multi-component signal to a single scalar, is a near-optimal spectral discriminant for tumor detection: across zones and estimators it ranks ROIs almost identically to the optimal eight-component classifier, because both concentrate on the two well-identified outer compartments (Figures~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant} and~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).

Rather than an impoverished summary of a richer signal, ADC effectively captures the dominant axis of spectral variation relevant to tumor detection.
The spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC; it explains it.
The added value of multi-component analysis lies in three areas: biological interpretability (which compartments---restricted diffusion, free water---drive the ADC contrast); uncertainty quantification (which components are reliably estimated); and the potential to surface grade-dependent spectral shifts that ADC compresses, such as glandular-water differences between Gleason grades.

The dominant free-diffusion bin at $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$---bulk water in gland lacunae of $\approx$100--300\,\textmu m---collapses in both tumor groups, consistent with replacement of patent lumina by solid tissue.
The $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ bin is more grade-specific: low-grade GGG\,$=\,1$ lesions retain near-normal glandular architecture and $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ mass, whereas the loss of glandular structure at higher Gleason patterns collapses this compartment too.
The redistribution of displaced spectral mass toward $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, most pronounced in GGG\,$\geq 3$, is consistent with the increased cell-packing density that is the morphological hallmark of higher-grade cancer, and appears to track the predominant-Gleason-pattern transition at which ADC-based grading is itself least reliable---although the small per-grade samples preclude a statistical claim.
Any incremental grading signal beyond ADC would have to come from the intermediate bins, where ADC is least sensitive (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}); but their wide posterior uncertainty (CV~$> 0.8$) and the small grade-specific samples preclude a statistical claim.
We present this histology mapping as a candidate interpretation the decomposition makes directly observable, not a validated finding; testing it would require a larger cohort with whole-mount histology correlation.

The comparison between MAP and NUTS estimation revealed a complementary relationship.
At tuned regularisation ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$), MAP and NUTS agreed closely for the well-identified components and their discriminant scores were nearly interchangeable ($r > 0.98$), confirming that closed-form MAP is sufficient for classification and recovers approximately the same fractions as the full sampler on realistic spectra.
The divergence between MAP and NUTS on intermediate components ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) was not a deficiency of either method but a faithful reflection of the ill-posedness: these components are genuinely undetermined by 15 b-value measurements, even at the much lower noise levels achieved with the endorectal coil and low-resolution acquisition of the present cohort.
NUTS makes this ill-posedness explicit through wide posterior intervals (CV $> 0.80$), reflecting the same Bayesian gain (effective estimation-error reduction relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound) that makes the well-identified components quantitatively trustworthy; MAP, in contrast, silently returns a point estimate that may be misleadingly precise.
The Bayesian approach thus identifies what can and cannot be learned from a given acquisition \citep{prange2009quantifying}.
Propagating the posterior through the classifier did not add diagnostic value beyond the point estimate (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}): consistent with the central finding, detection rides on the two well-identified outer compartments, leaving little estimation uncertainty in the discriminant direction to propagate. The posterior's value is therefore upstream---establishing which compartments are reliably recovered---not in the tumor-versus-normal decision itself.

The degeneracy of the intermediate components is a fundamental limit of the acquisition, not an artifact of the inference or the discretization: the Fisher correlations are intrinsic to the b-value sampling, the sampler reproduces the same smearing on simulated ground truths (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}), and re-fitting on grids that honor the $\Delta D \propto D^{3/2}$ spacing does not recover the lost components. What makes the well-constrained components quantitatively trustworthy is the two-step tightening of the achievable estimation error in Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b, which we read as two related gains: the prior contributes a regularization gain (the unconstrained-to-van-Trees step), and non-negativity contributes a further constraint gain (the van-Trees-to-NUTS step). Both act in the same direction---pulling the estimate toward the feasible, physically meaningful region---and together they reduce the effective estimation error by orders of magnitude relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er--Rao bound. Multi-exponential diffusion separation is itself ill-conditioned, with Cram\'er--Rao analyses showing severe noise propagation when the decaying components are close \citep{jalnefjord2019optimization}; the constrained Bayesian estimator sidesteps this by working in a far better-posed regime, reconciling that long-recognized difficulty with the recoverability we observe.

This study has several limitations.
First, the tumor ROIs were placed based on multiparametric MRI (including DWI/ADC maps), introducing a potential circularity: lesions that are well delineated on ADC maps may be overrepresented \citep{langkilde2018evaluation}.
This bias could inflate ADC's apparent discriminative performance relative to spectral features.
Only the Gleason grade classification, derived from biopsy pathology, is free from this circularity.
Second, the GGG sample size ($n = 29$) is insufficient for reliable AUC comparison between methods; differences below $\Delta\text{AUC} \approx 0.10$ cannot be meaningfully distinguished.
Third, the spectral model assumes a fixed set of diffusivity bins; a continuous spectral approach \citep{prange2009quantifying} might reveal additional structure but at the cost of increased ill-posedness.
Fourth, only one fixed echo-time/diffusion-time configuration was analyzed; the shorter echo and diffusion times enabled by recent gradient hardware \citep{zhu2024human, elsaid2026nonlinear} could change both the SNR and the relative weight of the compartments visible to the decomposition.
Finally, the pixel-wise demonstration (Figure~\ref{fig:pixelwise}) was limited to a single supplementary patient; generalization requires larger studies with histopathological correlation.
It also exposes a calibration caveat for per-voxel deployment: applying the ROI-trained detector voxel-wise yields scores that skew systematically toward the tumor class, because the substantially lower SNR of a single voxel relative to an averaged ROI inflates the restricted-diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) fraction.
This reinforces our decision to report all quantitative results at the ROI level, and indicates that faithful per-voxel spectral classification would require either explicit voxel-level noise modeling or spatial regularization.

The framework presented here is directly applicable to other organs and tumor types where multi-b DWI is acquired, including breast, liver, and brain tumors.
Amortized inference---training a neural network to approximate the posterior---could reduce computation from minutes per ROI to milliseconds, enabling real-time deployment, and the Fisher information framework could guide joint optimization of b-value protocols and discretization grids for specific clinical questions.
Integration with T\textsubscript{2}-weighted imaging could yield multi-parametric spectral biomarkers.
