\section{Discussion}
The central finding is that ADC, despite collapsing the multi-component signal to a single scalar, is a near-optimal spectral discriminant for tumor detection. Across zones and estimators it ranks ROIs almost identically to the optimal eight-component classifier (Figure~\ref{fig:adc_discriminant}).
Rather than an impoverished summary of a richer signal, ADC captures the dominant axis of spectral variation relevant to detection; the spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC, it explains it.
This is consistent with a recent comparison in which advanced diffusion models matched ADC for prostate cancer detection (@Stephan: this was also a finding of the Langkilde paper) while improving on it only for grade stratification \citep{he2025improved}.

\subsection{Why ADC works}
The near-equivalence has a simple mechanism (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).
ADC and the spectral discriminant are both, to first order, linear functionals of the same eight-bin spectrum: the monoexponential ADC is a smooth, monotonic-in-$D$ weighting of the fractions, and the discriminant is an explicit linear combination of them.
Across the cohort the tumor-versus-normal variation is concentrated in the two well-identified outer (@Stephan: see earlier comments about problem with outer bins description) bins---restricted diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rising as free water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) falls---so the spectra vary along an essentially one-dimensional axis.
Two linear readouts that both project onto that single axis must rank ROIs almost identically, which is exactly what we observe ($|r| > 0.97$).
The ADC sensitivity vector confirms the alignment: $\partial\text{ADC}/\partial R_j$ is large and of opposite sign on the two outer bins ($-1.59$ at $D = 0.25$, $+0.85$ at $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$), and its sign-flipped form tracks the logistic-regression coefficients ($r = -0.79$ PZ, $-0.88$ TZ), an agreement carried entirely by those two bins---only $D = 0.25$ and $3.0\;\umsmm$ have coefficients whose bootstrap intervals exclude zero.

This does not mean ADC ignores the intermediate compartments.

\subsection{Grade-dependent spectral shifts}
While detection collapses onto the two outer bins, any signal beyond ADC would have to live in the intermediate bins, where ADC is least sensitive---and this is plausibly where the decomposition adds value (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}).
The free-water bin ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$, a proxy for intact gland lumen) collapses as soon as tumor is present ($0.39$ normal to $0.21$ at GGG\,$=\,1$) and then changes little ($0.16$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$): it separates tumor from normal, not grade from grade.
By contrast the restricted-cellular bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) rises monotonically with grade ($0.07$ normal to $0.31$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$), and the glandular-epithelial bin ($D = 2.0\;\umsmm$) is preserved in low-grade tumor and depleted at higher grades ($0.26$ normal, $0.28$ at GGG\,$=\,1$, $0.17$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$).
This pattern follows the cellular reorganization of prostate adenocarcinoma---loss of patent lumina, glandular effacement, and rising cell-packing density---and is consistent with histological work relating epithelium, stroma, and lumen volumes to Gleason pattern \citep{chatterjee2015changes} (@Patrick: make sure we have good references here with a lot of ciattaions and recent), and with reports that advanced diffusion models improve grade differentiation where ADC plateaus \citep{johnston2019verdict, he2025improved}.
We present this as a candidate interpretation the decomposition makes directly observable, not a validated finding: the grade-specific samples are small ($n = 8$--$9$ per group) and preclude a statistical claim.

\subsection{What the Bayesian posterior contributes}
At tuned regularization ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$) the MAP point estimate and the NUTS posterior mean agreed closely on the well-identified components and gave nearly interchangeable discriminant scores ($r > 0.98$), so closed-form MAP is sufficient for classification.
Their divergence on the intermediate components is not a deficiency of either method but a faithful reflection of the ill-posedness: these fractions are genuinely underdetermined by fifteen b-values, even at the low noise of our endorectal-coil acquisition, and the sampler reproduces the same smearing on simulated ground truth (Figure~\ref{fig:validation}).
The posterior makes this explicit through a clear identifiability gradient: the three structurally distinct bins ($D = 0.25$, $3.0$, and $20\;\umsmm$) are well determined (posterior CV $0.20$--$0.36$), while the most collinear intermediate bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) are essentially unconstrained (CV $> 0.8$).
What makes the well-constrained components trustworthy is the two-step tightening of the achievable estimation error in Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b: the prior contributes a regularization gain and non-negativity a further constraint gain, both pulling the estimate toward the feasible region and together reducing the effective estimation error by orders of magnitude relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er--Rao bound.
Multi-exponential diffusion separation is intrinsically ill-conditioned \citep{jalnefjord2019optimization}; the constrained Bayesian estimator sidesteps this by working in a far better-posed regime.
The posterior's value is therefore upstream---establishing which compartments can be believed---rather than in the tumor-versus-normal decision itself.

Consistent with this, propagating the full posterior through the classifier added no diagnostic value beyond the point estimate (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}): detection rides on two well-identified outer compartments, leaving little estimation uncertainty in the discriminant direction to propagate, and supplying the per-component posterior standard deviations as additional features left detection AUC unchanged.
The credible intervals on $P(\mathrm{tumor})$ are on average $2.4\times$ wider for misclassified ROIs, but this is a geometric property of the logistic link---its slope is largest at the decision boundary---not a contribution of the spectral posterior: in logit space the difference is no longer significant.
Separately, the classifier returns a calibrated per-ROI probability rather than a hard label, and because errors concentrate near the boundary, distance from the boundary flags the least reliable predictions---a useful triage signal.
This prediction confidence, however, is a standard property of the logistic model, available from the point estimate alone and shared by the ADC- and spectrum-based classifiers, not a benefit of Bayesian inference (@Stephan: Nice that you explain this!).

\subsection{Pixel-wise feasibility}
The framework extends to the voxel level (Figure~\ref{fig:pixelwise}).
On a single peripheral-zone slice the restricted-diffusion ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) fraction map reproduced the ADC pattern and the free-water ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) map was its negative, and the per-voxel discriminant anti-correlated with ADC ($r = -0.97$); the decomposition additionally provided a per-voxel uncertainty map, with the eight-bin detector $1.8\times$ more uncertain than the two-bin detector because it weights the unidentifiable intermediate compartments.
This per-voxel demonstration also exposes a calibration caveat: the much lower SNR of a single voxel inflates the restricted fraction, biasing the ROI-trained detector toward the tumor class, which reinforces our decision to report all quantitative results at the ROI level.
We present the pixel-wise maps as an exploratory feasibility demonstration; the uncertainty does not yet propagate to improved detection, but it does indicate which spectral compartments are locally reliable.
Three-direction acquisition in another patient cohort confirmed that the decomposition is direction-independent at the diagnostically dominant restricted bin (per-direction coefficient of variation $\approx 9\%$; Figure~\ref{fig:directions} (@Stephan: This probably has to become Fig S3, since it is the last of these figures you mention (not to be found in Methods or Results).)), supporting the use of trace-averaged signal.

\subsection{Limitations and future directions}
This study has several limitations.
First, the tumor ROIs were placed on multiparametric MRI, including DWI and ADC maps, introducing a potential circularity: lesions conspicuous on ADC may be overrepresented \citep{langkilde2018evaluation}, which could inflate ADC's apparent performance relative to spectral features.
Compounding this, the detection-positive ROIs are radiological targets rather than biopsy-confirmed cancers---11 of the 40 were not confirmed malignant on biopsy (7 benign, 4 ungraded)---so the detection contrast is partly against the radiologist's own ADC-informed reading; only the Gleason grade analysis, derived from biopsy pathology, is free of this circularity.
Second, the graded sample ($n = 29$) is too small for a reliable between-method comparison of grading performance.
Third, the spectral model assumes a fixed diffusivity grid; a continuous spectrum \citep{prange2009quantifying} might reveal additional structure at the cost of greater ill-posedness.
Fourth, a single echo-time and diffusion-time configuration was analyzed; the shorter timings enabled by recent gradient hardware \citep{zhu2024human, elsaid2026nonlinear} could change both the SNR and the relative weight of the visible compartments.
Finally, the pixel-wise result rests on a single patient and requires larger studies with histopathological correlation.

The framework applies directly to other organs and tumor types in which multi-b DWI is acquired, such as breast, liver, and brain.
Amortized inference---training a neural network to approximate the posterior---could reduce computation from minutes per ROI to milliseconds, and the Fisher-information analysis could guide joint optimization of b-value protocols and diffusivity grids for a given clinical question.
Integration with quantitative T\textsubscript{2} mapping, which independently resolves luminal water \citep{sabouri2017luminal}, could yield multiparametric spectral biomarkers that separate the grading axis from the detection axis characterized here.
