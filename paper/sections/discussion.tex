Two compartments of the prostate diffusivity spectrum---restricted intracellular water and free luminal water---are reliably recoverable from a clinical multi-b acquisition, and they move together as cancer raises cellular density. This is the mechanistic reason ADC is hard to beat for detection: the discriminative signal is essentially one-dimensional, and ADC reads out exactly that contrast. The long-standing tension that the simplest possible diffusion model competes with far richer ones \citep{bourne2016limitations, si2018diagnostic, fennessy2023quantitative} is thus not a paradox but a consequence of what the data contain---a point prior work approached by noting that advanced parameters correlate with ADC \citep{he2025improved}, which our decomposition makes precise: ADC and the optimal eight-component classifier rank ROIs almost identically because both are dominated by the same two outer compartments. The value of the full spectrum is therefore not a better detector but an explanation, an explicit account of which compartments drive the contrast, and per-component uncertainty.

\subsection{Reconciling Two Literatures: Detection versus Grading}

Reports that advanced diffusion models do or do not beat ADC can be reconciled by separating the task. For \emph{detection}, a properly lesion-localized, PI-RADS-compliant ADC is hard to outperform \citep{fennessy2023quantitative, he2025improved, si2018diagnostic}, consistent with the two-compartment collapse we observe. The large detection gaps reported for restriction spectrum imaging (ADC AUC $\approx 0.5$) arise not from b-value choice but from an automated whole-gland \emph{minimum}-ADC that does not localize the lesion as a radiologist would \citep{zhong2022automated}; against a localized ADC the gap largely closes. For \emph{grading}, by contrast, cellular-compartment metrics such as VERDICT intracellular volume fraction genuinely separate Gleason grades where ADC does not \citep{johnston2019verdict, singh2022innovate}. Our spectrum shows why both hold at once: grading signal sits in the intermediate and glandular diffusivities (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}) that the two-compartment detection axis does not span---and that, as shown below, the data resolve least precisely.

\subsection{Identifiability and the Multi-compartment Literature}

That intermediate diffusivity components are poorly determined is consistent with the broader finding that multi-compartment diffusion models are degenerate, fitting parameters the data underconstrain \citep{jelescu2016degeneracy, novikov2018modeling, lampinen2019searching}, a hazard raised specifically for prostate multi-exponential analysis \citep{mulkern2017perils}. Our contribution is to make this quantitative for a clinical prostate acquisition, from two independent directions---Fisher information and the full posterior---that agree: only the two outer compartments are well identified, the glandular bins carry large absolute posterior uncertainty even where their fractions are large, and this is robust to prior width and reproduced in simulation.
This refines, rather than contradicts, model-selection studies that favor more compartments. A four-compartment model can minimize a penalized population criterion \citep{conlin2021improved} while the individual intermediate fractions remain undetermined in any single ROI: model selection asks how many components improve the aggregate fit, whereas identifiability asks whether a given fraction can be estimated from one measurement. The practical implication is that intermediate-diffusivity parameters from such fits should be interpreted with explicit uncertainty, or constrained, rather than read as point values.

\subsection{What the Bayesian Posterior Provides}

At the tuned regularization, MAP and NUTS yield equivalent spectra and nearly interchangeable discriminant scores ($r > 0.98$), so the closed-form MAP is a fast and sufficient point estimator for classification. The full posterior is the complementary, exploratory tool: it infers the noise level jointly rather than assuming it \citep{sjolund2018bayesian}, and it returns per-component uncertainty that quantifies which compartments are trustworthy---the relaxometry-spectrum lineage of \citet{whittall1989quantitative} and \citet{prange2009quantifying}, here applied to prostate diffusion (cf.\ \citet{wells2022estimation}). The unconstrained Cram\'er--Rao bound vastly overstates the achievable error; incorporating the half-normal prior and the non-negativity constraint reduces it by orders of magnitude (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b), which is why a problem that looks hopeless under classical analysis is in practice tractable.

The posterior can be carried through to the decision itself: propagating each ROI's spectral posterior through the classifier yields a calibrated cancer probability with a credible interval whose width is larger for misclassified than for correctly classified ROIs (Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}), an internal confidence flag obtained at no additional cost. Uncertainty-based flagging of unreliable predictions is established in medical-image deep learning \citep{ghesu2021quantifying, tanno2021uncertainty} and increasingly called for in radiologic AI \citep{faghani2023quantifying, alves2023prediction}; to our knowledge it has not been driven by a biophysical diffusion posterior in prostate, and we present it as an exploratory illustration of that capability.

\subsection{A Candidate Histological Reading of Grade}

The grade-dependent shifts admit a coherent, if hypothesis-generating, histological reading \citep{hectors2018advanced}. The dominant free-diffusion bin ($D = 3.0\;\umsmm$), plausibly bulk water in gland lumina, collapses once tumor replaces patent lumina, separating tumor from normal but saturating thereafter. The glandular bin ($D = 2.0\;\umsmm$) is retained in low-grade tumor with near-normal architecture and depletes as glandular structure is lost at higher grades, while mass shifts to the restricted bin ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$) with increasing cell-packing density---the morphological hallmark of higher grade. \stephan{please sanity-check this histological mapping and point me to the best references for the lumen / glandular / cellular interpretation.} That the grade-relevant compartments are the least precisely resolved is exactly why a larger cohort with whole-mount histology would be needed to test it.

\subsection{Limitations}

Tumor ROIs were placed from multiparametric MRI, which includes DWI, raising a potential circularity; in practice lesions are localized from T\textsubscript{2}-weighted and DWI visual contrast rather than quantitative ADC values, limiting but not eliminating this bias, and only the biopsy-derived grade analysis is free of it. The grade analysis is itself limited by sample size ($n = 29$) and is reported as exploratory. A single fixed echo- and diffusion-time configuration was analyzed; the shorter timings enabled by recent gradient hardware \citep{zhu2024human, elsaid2026nonlinear} could change both the SNR and the compartment weights. The pixel-wise demonstration used one slice; applying the ROI-trained detector voxel-wise skews scores tumor-ward because the lower per-voxel SNR inflates the restricted fraction, so faithful per-voxel classification would require voxel-level noise modeling or spatial regularization---reinforcing the ROI-level scope of the quantitative results. Finally, the posterior is well calibrated on smooth spectra but over-confident on concentrated $\delta$-spectra, which are unrepresentative of prostate tissue but bound the calibration claim.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Amortized or variational inference could reduce computation from minutes to milliseconds per ROI, enabling real-time deployment, and the Fisher framework could jointly optimize b-value protocols and discretization grids for a target compartment. The approach extends to other organs where multi-b DWI is acquired, and integration with T\textsubscript{2}-weighted imaging could yield multiparametric spectral biomarkers.
