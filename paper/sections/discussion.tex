\section{Discussion}

The central finding of this study is that ADC, despite reducing multi-component diffusion information to a single scalar, achieves near-optimal spectral discrimination for prostate tumor detection.
The anti-correlation between ADC scores and the optimal spectral discriminant ($r \approx -0.98$ across zones and inference methods) is not coincidental: the sensitivity analysis shows that ADC's implicit weighting of spectral compartments closely matches the learned classification direction (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}).
This means that the rank ordering of patients by ADC is, to a very close approximation, identical to the rank ordering by the optimal eight-component spectral classifier---ADC is already performing the same discrimination as the full spectral model.
This provides a formal, quantitative answer to what ADC measures in the context of spectral tissue characterization: it is a near-optimal linear combination of the diffusivity spectrum for the purpose of distinguishing tumor from normal prostate tissue.

This result has important implications for the clinical interpretation of ADC.
Rather than viewing ADC as an impoverished summary of a richer signal, our analysis suggests that it effectively captures the dominant axis of spectral variation relevant to tumor detection.
The spectral decomposition does not obsolete ADC; it explains it.
The added value of multi-component analysis lies in three areas: (1)~biological interpretability, revealing which tissue compartments (restricted diffusion, free water) drive the observed ADC contrast; (2)~uncertainty quantification, identifying which components are reliably estimated from the available data; and (3)~potential for detecting spectral signatures that are orthogonal to the dominant tumor--normal axis, such as differences in glandular water content between Gleason grades.
We note that ADC can also be computed without the $b = 0$ image, in which the perfusion contribution is most prominent, and that prior work has shown that this can yield slightly different discrimination performance, sometimes better and sometimes worse depending on cohort and protocol \citep{aksitciris2019accelerated}.
Our analysis follows PI-RADS recommendations and includes $b = 0$, which makes the resulting ADC sensitive to the vascular ($D = 20\;\umsmm$) compartment via the intercept term; a more deliberate exclusion of the perfusion-dominated b-value could be examined in future work.

A candidate histological interpretation of the grade-dependent spectral shifts (Figure~\ref{fig:spectrum_ggg}) follows from the cellular reorganization characteristic of prostate adenocarcinoma.
The dominant free-diffusion bin at $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$---physically consistent with bulk water in gland lacunae of approximately 100--300\,\textmu m diameter---collapses in both tumor groups, consistent with replacement of patent gland lumina by solid tissue.
The differential behaviour of the $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ bin is more grade-specific: low-grade GGG\,$=\,1$ lesions, defined by preserved well-formed or cribriform glandular architecture, retain the $D = 2.0\;\umsmm$ mass at near-normal levels, whereas the loss of glandular structure that defines higher-grade Gleason patterns is associated with collapse of this compartment as well.
The redistribution of displaced spectral mass toward $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$, most pronounced in GGG\,$\geq 2$, is consistent with the increase in cell-packing density that is the morphological hallmark of higher-grade prostate cancer; within the GGG\,$\geq 2$ group this elevation was concentrated in the GGG\,$\geq 3$ subset, with the group-mean restricted-cellular fraction rising from $\approx 0.17$ at both GGG\,$=\,1$ and GGG\,$=\,2$ to $\approx 0.31$ at GGG\,$\geq 3$ ($n = 9$), so the restricted-water fraction appears to track the predominant-Gleason-pattern transition at which ADC-based grading is itself least reliable---although the small per-grade samples preclude a statistical claim.
The mild elevation of the intermediate bins ($D = 0.5$--$1.5\;\umsmm$) in GGG\,$\geq 2$ relative to GGG\,$=\,1$ is intriguing because these are precisely the diffusivities at which ADC is least sensitive (Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}), suggesting that any incremental grading signal beyond ADC would have to come from this region; however, the grade-specific sample sizes ($n = 8$ and $n = 21$) and the wide per-bin posterior uncertainty for intermediate components (CV~$> 0.8$) preclude any statistical claim.
We therefore present this histology mapping as a candidate biological interpretation that the spectral decomposition makes directly observable, not as a validated finding; a larger cohort with whole-mount histology correlation would be required to test it.

The comparison between MAP and NUTS estimation revealed a complementary relationship.
At tuned regularisation ($\lambda = 10^{-3}$), MAP and NUTS agreed closely for the well-identified components ($D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ and $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$) and their discriminant scores were nearly interchangeable ($r > 0.98$), confirming that closed-form MAP estimation is sufficient for classification and recovers approximately the same compartment fractions as the full Bayesian sampler on realistic spectra.
The original divergence reported at $\lambda = 0.1$ (MAP systematically underestimating the $D = 3.0\;\umsmm$ fraction by approximately 35\,\%) is therefore best understood as a regularisation artifact rather than an intrinsic property of point estimation: at sufficiently small $\lambda$, the ridge penalty no longer enforces smoothing of dominant bins into adjacent ones, and the same constrained quadratic program (Equation~\ref{eq:map}) recovers the dominant lumen fraction.
The same ridge-smoothing phenomenon explains why the discrete four-compartment fits of \citet{conlin2021improved} are not directly comparable to MAP fractions estimated at heavier regularisation on overlapping bins.
The divergence between MAP and NUTS on intermediate components ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) was not a deficiency of either method but a faithful reflection of the ill-posedness: these components are genuinely undetermined by 15 b-value measurements, even at the much lower noise levels achieved with the endorectal coil and low-resolution acquisition of the present cohort.
NUTS makes this ill-posedness explicit through wide posterior intervals (CV $> 0.80$), reflecting the same Bayesian gain (effective estimation-error reduction relative to the unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao bound) that makes the well-identified components quantitatively trustworthy; MAP, in contrast, silently returns a point estimate that may be misleadingly precise.
The Bayesian approach thus serves as an exploratory tool that identifies what can and cannot be learned from a given acquisition protocol---a perspective endorsed by the information-theoretic view of inverse problems \citep{prange2009quantifying}.
Misclassified ROIs in the NUTS-based predictions also showed elevated posterior uncertainty (1.2--1.3$\times$ higher mean spectral CV than correctly classified samples; Figure~\ref{fig:uncertainty}), suggesting that posterior uncertainty provides an intrinsic, model-internal quality metric for classification confidence.

The Fisher information analysis (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}) provides a theoretical foundation for the observed identifiability pattern.
The Fisher correlation matrix reveals that intermediate diffusivity components are mutually correlated above $r = 0.99$, making them fundamentally indistinguishable regardless of the estimation method.
The Cram\'er-Rao analysis yields a concrete design principle: in the two-compartment limit, the minimum resolvable bin spacing scales as $\Delta D \propto D^{3/2}$ (Equation~\ref{eq:spacing_scaling}), meaning resolution degrades rapidly with increasing diffusivity.
Our grid approximately follows this scaling, but numerical evaluation of the full $8 \times 8$ Fisher matrix shows that no rearrangement of bins eliminates the ill-conditioning of intermediate components---it merely shifts the problem along the diffusivity axis.
This confirms that the poor identifiability of the $D = 0.50$--$1.00\;\umsmm$ range is an intrinsic property of the Laplace inversion at the SNR realized here, not an artifact of suboptimal discretization.
The unconstrained Cram\'er-Rao lower bound predicts estimation errors 100--8000$\times$ larger than what the Bayesian sampler achieves, demonstrating that non-negativity constraints and prior information are not merely convenient regularization but are essential for making the spectral decomposition tractable; this resolves an apparent discrepancy between earlier CRLB-based pessimism about multi-compartment fitting and the practical recoverability we observe.

This study has several limitations.
First, the tumor ROIs were placed based on multiparametric MRI (including DWI/ADC maps), introducing a potential circularity: lesions that are well delineated on ADC maps may be overrepresented \citep{langkilde2018evaluation}.
This bias could inflate ADC's apparent discriminative performance relative to spectral features.
Only the Gleason grade classification, derived from biopsy pathology, is free from this circularity.
Second, the GGG sample size ($n = 29$) is insufficient for reliable AUC comparison between methods; differences below $\Delta\text{AUC} \approx 0.10$ cannot be meaningfully distinguished.
Third, the spectral model assumes a fixed set of diffusivity bins; a continuous spectral approach \citep{prange2009quantifying} might reveal additional structure but at the cost of increased ill-posedness.
Fourth, only data acquired with one fixed configuration of echo time and diffusion time was analyzed; recent advances in gradient performance allow substantially shorter echo and diffusion times \citep{zhu2024human, elsaid2026nonlinear}, which could change both the absolute SNR and the relative weight of the various diffusion compartments visible to the spectral decomposition.
Finally, the pixel-wise demonstration was limited to a single supplementary patient; generalization requires larger studies with histopathological correlation.

The framework presented here is directly applicable to other organs and tumor types where multi-b DWI is acquired, including breast, liver, and brain tumors.
Amortized inference approaches---training a neural network to approximate the posterior distribution---could reduce computation time from minutes per ROI (NUTS) to milliseconds, enabling real-time clinical deployment.
While the $D^{3/2}$ scaling law shows that bin redistribution alone cannot overcome the intrinsic ill-posedness, the Fisher information framework could guide joint optimization of b-value protocols and discretization grids to maximize identifiability for specific clinical questions.
Integration with T\textsubscript{2}-weighted imaging could yield multi-parametric spectral biomarkers.
