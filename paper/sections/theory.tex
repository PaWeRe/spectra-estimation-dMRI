\section{Theory}

\subsection{Signal Model}

In multi-b diffusion MRI the signal decay is modeled as a superposition of exponential decays from distinct diffusion compartments. One can either estimate the diffusivities $D_j$ and their fractions jointly---highly nonlinear and severely non-unique---or fix the diffusivities on a predefined grid and estimate only the fractions, reducing the problem to linear estimation at the cost of discretization. We adopt the latter: normalizing by the $b = 0$ signal, the discrete spectral model is
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{S(b_i)}{S_0} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} R_j \exp(-b_i D_j) + \varepsilon_i,
  \quad i = 1, \ldots, M,
  \label{eq:signal_model}
\end{equation}
%
where $R_j \geq 0$ are the spectral fractions (volume fractions of each diffusion compartment), $D_j$ are the diffusivity bin centers, and $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is additive Gaussian noise.
In matrix notation, $\svec / S_0 = \Umat \Rvec + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, where the design matrix $\Umat \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times K}$ has entries $U_{ij} = \exp(-b_i D_j)$.

For this study, $M = 15$ b-values spanning $0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ in steps of $250\;\text{s/mm}^2$ and $K = 8$ diffusivity bins at $D \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 20.0\}\;\umsmm$.
The design matrix $\Umat$ has condition number $\kappa(\Umat) \approx 2.8 \times 10^5$, reflecting the inherent ill-posedness of the inverse problem.

\subsection{Identifiability and Discretization}

The choice of diffusivity bins $D_j$ determines which spectral components can be resolved. The Fisher information matrix for the fractions $\Rvec$ (assuming known noise $\sigma$) is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \Umat^T \Umat,
  \quad F_{jk} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \exp\!\big(-b_i(D_j + D_k)\big).
  \label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
%
The Cram\'er-Rao lower bound (CRLB) provides the minimum achievable variance for any unbiased estimator: $\text{Var}(\hat{R}_j) \geq [\mathbf{F}^{-1}]_{jj}$.
Because $\mathbf{F} = \Umat^T\Umat/\sigma^2$, its condition number is the square of the design matrix's, $\kappa(\mathbf{F}) = \kappa(\Umat)^2 \approx 8 \times 10^{10}$---the same ill-posedness expressed in estimation-variance terms.

The off-diagonal structure of $\mathbf{F}$ reveals why: adjacent diffusivity components produce nearly identical signal contributions.
The Fisher correlation matrix $C_{jk} = F_{jk} / \sqrt{F_{jj} F_{kk}}$ shows that components at $D = 0.50$, $0.75$, and $1.00\;\umsmm$ are correlated above $r = 0.99$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a), making them fundamentally indistinguishable from the 15 b-value measurements considered here.
In contrast, $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ (restricted diffusion) contributes detectable signal across all 15 b-values, while fast-diffusing components ($D \geq 3.0$) decay below the noise floor within the first few b-value steps (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}c).

This resolving limitation can be quantified analytically for a two-compartment toy model.
Consider adjacent diffusivities $D$ and $D + \Delta D$ contributing fractions $p$ and $1-p$.
In the asymptotic regime $\Delta D \ll D$ and $b_{\max} D \gg 1$, the Fisher information for $p$ scales as
%
\begin{equation}
  I \propto \frac{(\Delta D)^2}{D^3}.
  \label{eq:fisher_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
Requiring constant estimation precision across the spectrum therefore implies a minimum resolvable bin spacing that grows as
%
\begin{equation}
  \Delta D \propto D^{3/2}.
  \label{eq:spacing_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
We use this scaling as a heuristic guide for the discretization: our grid spacing increases from $\Delta D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ at the slow-diffusion end to $1.0\;\umsmm$ at $D = 2.0$, placing denser bins where most b-values contribute informative signal.
Numerical inversion of the full $8 \times 8$ Fisher matrix confirms that intermediate components ($D = 0.50$--$1.50\;\umsmm$) remain poorly conditioned regardless of bin placement: the $D^{3/2}$ redistribution only trades resolution between the two ends, and the ill-posedness is intrinsic to the Laplace inversion.

The unconstrained CRLB vastly overestimates the actual estimation error because it ignores the prior: for intermediate components it predicts standard deviations exceeding the $[0,1]$ range of a volume fraction (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b).

\subsection{MAP Estimation}

The maximum a posteriori estimate under a Gaussian (ridge) prior $R_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^{-1})$ combined with the physical non-negativity of compartment fractions is the solution of the constrained quadratic program
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Rvec}_{\text{MAP}} \;=\; \argmin_{\Rvec \,\in\, \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{K}}
  \Big\{\; \big\lVert\, \svec/S_0 \;-\; \Umat\,\Rvec\, \big\rVert_2^{\,2}
  \;+\; \lambda\, \lVert \Rvec \rVert_2^{\,2}\, \Big\},
  \label{eq:map}
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda > 0$ controls the regularization strength and the feasible set $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{K}$ enforces non-negativity of the compartment fractions.
For this ill-conditioned design matrix, the unconstrained Gaussian MAP $(\Umat^{T}\Umat + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1}\Umat^{T}(\svec/S_0)$ can lie outside the feasible orthant, and projecting that closed-form solution onto $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{K}$ does not in general recover the constrained MAP in (\ref{eq:map}).
We therefore solve (\ref{eq:map}) directly as non-negative least squares on the augmented system $[\Umat;\, \sqrt{\lambda}\,\mathbf{I}]\,\Rvec = [\svec/S_0;\, \mathbf{0}]$, which is equivalent to the original ridge-NNLS objective and is solved with an active-set algorithm.
The MAP estimate is a point estimate and does not quantify how reliably each component $R_j$ is determined by the data.

\subsection{Bayesian Formulation}

Beyond offering no measure of per-component reliability, the MAP estimate requires the noise level $\sigma$ to be known, and both limitations bite here: a point estimate at an intermediate component may appear precise while being largely determined by the regularization rather than the data, and an incorrect $\sigma$ silently rescales the penalty.
A fully Bayesian approach characterizes the joint posterior over $(\Rvec, \sigma)$, inferring the spectrum and per-ROI noise level together and making the ill-posedness explicit through the marginal posterior widths.

We specify a hierarchical model with explicit priors on both the spectrum and the noise level:
%
\begin{align}
  R_j &\sim \text{HalfNormal}\!\left(\sigma_R = 1/\sqrt{\lambda}\right),
  \quad j = 1, \ldots, K \label{eq:prior_R} \\
  \sigma &\sim \text{HalfCauchy}(\beta = 0.01) \label{eq:prior_sigma} \\
  \svec / S_0 \mid \Rvec, \sigma &\sim \mathcal{N}(\Umat \Rvec,\; \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}).
  \label{eq:likelihood}
\end{align}
%
The half-normal prior on $\Rvec$ enforces non-negativity with smooth gradients for Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), and its variance $1/\lambda$ matches the ridge penalty, linking the MAP and Bayesian formulations. The half-Cauchy prior on $\sigma$ is a robust weakly informative choice \citep{gelman2006prior} that lets the noise be inferred jointly rather than fixed a priori.

The posterior $p(\Rvec, \sigma \mid \svec, \Umat)$ is sampled using the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts}.
A coordinate-wise Gibbs sampler with conjugate full conditionals targets the same posterior, but the strong correlations between adjacent spectral components (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a) create a posterior geometry in which such coordinate-wise updates mix slowly; we therefore use NUTS.
NUTS, as an adaptive variant of HMC \citep{neal2011mcmc}, exploits gradient information to propose joint moves across all components simultaneously and adapts the trajectory length to the local curvature of the posterior, making it well suited to the correlated, constrained geometry of this problem.

Posterior summaries for each $R_j$ provide both a point estimate and a measure of identifiability via the coefficient of variation (CV = posterior std / posterior mean): components with CV $\ll 1$ are well constrained, those with CV near or above one effectively undetermined by the data.

\subsection{ADC as a Spectral Functional}

The apparent diffusion coefficient can be viewed as a functional of the spectral fractions.
Given a spectrum $\Rvec$, the synthesized signal at each b-value is $\hat{S}(b) = \sum_j R_j \exp(-b D_j)$.
Fitting a monoexponential $\log \hat{S}(b) = \log \hat{S}_0 - b \cdot \text{ADC}$ to b-values below a cutoff $b_{\max}$ yields the apparent diffusion coefficient $\text{ADC}(\Rvec)$.
Log-domain least-squares fitting introduces a known noise-distribution bias at low SNR \citep{kuczera2023truly}, but is the clinical standard and is adopted here for comparability with the conventional ADC pipeline.
This is a nonlinear, spectrum-dependent mapping: the same diffusivity component $R_j$ contributes differently to ADC depending on the operating point (i.e., the overall spectral composition).

To understand which spectral components ADC is most sensitive to, we define the sensitivity vector
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \,\text{ADC}}{\partial R_j}\bigg|_{\Rvec = \bar{\Rvec}}
  \approx \frac{\text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec} + \epsilon \, \mathbf{e}_j) - \text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec})}{\epsilon},
  \label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}
%
evaluated at a reference spectrum $\bar{\Rvec}$ (e.g., the average tumor or normal spectrum).
This sensitivity vector quantifies how a unit increase in each spectral fraction perturbs the ADC value.
If the sensitivity vector aligns with the optimal classification direction (the logistic-regression coefficient vector), then ranking by ADC approximates ranking by the optimal spectral classifier; we test this alignment directly (Results).
We quantify this alignment using the Pearson correlation between the sensitivity vector $\partial \text{ADC}/\partial \Rvec$ and the logistic-regression coefficient vector $\mathbf{w}$ (fit on standardized fractions).
