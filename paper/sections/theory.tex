\section{Theory}

\subsection{Signal Model and Discretization}

In multi-b diffusion MRI, the signal decay can be modeled as a superposition of exponential decays from distinct diffusion compartments. One can either estimate the diffusivities $D_j$ and their fractions jointly---highly nonlinear and severely non-unique---or fix the diffusivities on a predefined grid and estimate only the fractions, reducing the problem to linear estimation at the cost of discretization. We adopt the latter: normalizing by the $b = 0$ signal, the discrete spectral model is
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{S(b_i)}{S_0} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} R_j \exp(-b_i D_j) + \varepsilon_i,
  \quad i = 1, \ldots, M,
  \label{eq:signal_model}
\end{equation}
%
where $R_j \geq 0$ are the spectral fractions (volume fractions of each diffusion compartment), $D_j$ are the diffusivity bin centers, and $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is additive Gaussian noise.
In matrix notation, $\svec / S_0 = \Umat \Rvec + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, where the design matrix $\Umat \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times K}$ has entries $U_{ij} = \exp(-b_i D_j)$.
For this study, $M = 15$ b-values span $0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ in steps of $250\;\text{s/mm}^2$ and $K = 8$ diffusivity bins at $D \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 20.0\}\;\umsmm$.

The conditioning of the design matrix can guide the choice of the diffusivity grid.
Assuming known noise $\sigma$, the Fisher information matrix for the fractions $\Rvec$ is
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \Umat^T \Umat,
  \quad F_{jk} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \exp\!\big(-b_i(D_j + D_k)\big),
  \label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
%
and the Cram\'er--Rao lower bound (CRLB) sets the minimum variance of any unbiased estimator, $\text{Var}(\hat{R}_j) \geq [\mathbf{F}^{-1}]_{jj}$.
The design matrix is ill-conditioned ($\kappa(\Umat) \approx 2.8 \times 10^5$), and because $\mathbf{F} = \Umat^T\Umat/\sigma^2$ this becomes its square in estimation-variance terms ($\kappa(\mathbf{F}) = \kappa(\Umat)^2 \approx 8 \times 10^{10}$).
The large off-diagonal entries of $\mathbf{F}$ reflect that adjacent diffusivities produce nearly identical signal decays: the low- and intermediate-diffusivity components ($D = 0.5$--$1.0\;\umsmm$) are mutually correlated above $0.99$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a) and trade off against one another almost freely, whereas $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ contributes signal across all 15 b-values and components with $D \geq 3.0\;\umsmm$ fall below the noise floor within the first few b-value steps (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}c).

A two-compartment analysis indicates where on the grid this trade-off is least severe.
For adjacent diffusivities $D$ and $D + \Delta D$ carrying fractions $p$ and $1-p$, in the regime $\Delta D \ll D$ and $b_{\max} D \gg 1$ the Fisher information for $p$ scales as
%
\begin{equation}
  I \propto \frac{(\Delta D)^2}{D^3},
  \label{eq:fisher_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
so holding precision constant across the spectrum would require a bin spacing that grows as
%
\begin{equation}
  \Delta D \propto D^{3/2},
  \label{eq:spacing_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
that is, narrower bins are supported at low diffusivity.
We treat this analytic rule as a heuristic and combine it with biological knowledge of the relevant water compartments to set the eight-bin grid above.

\subsection{Bayesian Spectrum Estimation}

We treat spectrum recovery as Bayesian inference. The Gaussian observation model (\ref{eq:signal_model}), the physical constraint $R_j \geq 0$, and a regularizing prior on the fractions together define a posterior over the spectrum (and, in the full treatment, the noise level $\sigma$). Two estimators follow from this single posterior: its mode---the maximum a posteriori (MAP) point estimate---and the full posterior itself, sampled to additionally quantify how reliably each component is determined.

Under a Gaussian (ridge) prior $R_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^{-1})$ together with non-negativity, the MAP estimate solves the constrained quadratic program (@Stephan: equation rather than program?, @patrick: I am not sure program is actually the better term here, as this is what the approach is called or not?)
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Rvec}_{\text{MAP}} \;=\; \argmin_{\Rvec \,\in\, \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{K}}
  \Big\{\; \big\lVert\, \svec/S_0 \;-\; \Umat\,\Rvec\, \big\rVert_2^{\,2}
  \;+\; \lambda\, \lVert \Rvec \rVert_2^{\,2}\, \Big\},
  \label{eq:map}
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda > 0$ sets the regularization strength.
For this ill-conditioned design matrix the unconstrained Gaussian MAP $(\Umat^{T}\Umat + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1}\Umat^{T}(\svec/S_0)$ can lie outside the feasible orthant, so projecting it onto $\mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}^{K}$ does not in general recover the constrained solution; we instead solve (\ref{eq:map}) directly as non-negative least squares on the augmented system $[\Umat;\, \sqrt{\lambda}\,\mathbf{I}]\,\Rvec = [\svec/S_0;\, \mathbf{0}]$ with an active-set algorithm.
The MAP estimate requires no noise level ($\lambda$, not $\sigma$, governs the bias--variance trade-off) but gives no measure of per-component reliability: a value at a poorly conditioned component may appear precise while being largely set by the regularization rather than the data.

The full Bayesian treatment infers the spectrum and per-ROI noise level jointly, making the ill-posedness explicit through the marginal posterior widths.
We specify a hierarchical model,
%
\begin{align}
  R_j &\sim \text{HalfNormal}\!\left(\sigma_R\right),
  \quad j = 1, \ldots, K \label{eq:prior_R} \\
  \sigma &\sim \text{HalfCauchy}(\beta = 0.01) \label{eq:prior_sigma} \\
  \svec / S_0 \mid \Rvec, \sigma &\sim \mathcal{N}(\Umat \Rvec,\; \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}),
  \label{eq:likelihood}
\end{align}
%
in which the half-normal prior enforces non-negativity with smooth gradients---the Bayesian analogue of the ridge penalty, though the prior scale $\sigma_R$ and the ridge weight $\lambda$ are separate hyperparameters tuned independently (Methods)---and the half-Cauchy is a robust weakly informative prior that lets the noise be inferred jointly \citep{gelman2006prior}.
We sample the posterior $p(\Rvec, \sigma \mid \svec, \Umat)$ with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo \citep{neal2011mcmc}, which augments the parameters with auxiliary momenta and simulates gradient-driven trajectories of the log-posterior to propose distant, jointly-updated states that retain a high acceptance probability; the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts} removes the need to hand-tune the trajectory length. We also implemented a coordinate-wise Gibbs sampler with conjugate full conditionals, but the strong correlations between adjacent components made it mix too slowly, so all results use NUTS.
Each posterior summary then provides both a point estimate and a per-component coefficient of variation (CV = posterior std / posterior mean) as a measure of identifiability: the smaller the CV, the more tightly the data constrain that fraction, whereas a CV approaching one marks a fraction the data barely determine.

Together, the regularization and the non-negativity constraint are what render this classically ill-posed inversion tractable. At a representative SNR the unconstrained CRLB is large---for the intermediate bins it exceeds the $[0,1]$ range a fraction can occupy---whereas approximating the half-normal prior by a Gaussian (the van Trees bound) tightens the achievable variance by up to two orders of magnitude, and enforcing non-negativity tightens it by a further $2$--$74\times$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b).

\subsection{ADC as a Spectral Functional}

The apparent diffusion coefficient can be viewed as a functional of the spectral fractions.
Given a spectrum $\Rvec$, the synthesized signal at each b-value is $\hat{S}(b) = \sum_j R_j \exp(-b D_j)$.
Fitting a monoexponential $\log \hat{S}(b) = \log \hat{S}_0 - b \cdot \text{ADC}$ to b-values below a cutoff $b_{\max}$ yields the apparent diffusion coefficient $\text{ADC}(\Rvec)$.
Log-domain least-squares fitting introduces a known noise-distribution bias at low SNR \citep{kuczera2023truly}, but is the clinical standard and is adopted here for comparability with the conventional ADC pipeline.
This is a nonlinear, spectrum-dependent mapping: the same diffusivity component $R_j$ contributes differently to ADC depending on the operating point (i.e., the overall spectral composition).

To understand which spectral components ADC is most sensitive to, we define the sensitivity vector
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \,\text{ADC}}{\partial R_j}\bigg|_{\Rvec = \bar{\Rvec}}
  \approx \frac{\text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec} + \epsilon \, \mathbf{e}_j) - \text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec})}{\epsilon},
  \label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}
%
evaluated at a reference spectrum $\bar{\Rvec}$ (e.g., the average tumor or normal spectrum).
This sensitivity vector quantifies how a unit increase in each spectral fraction perturbs the ADC value.
If the sensitivity vector aligns with the optimal classification direction (the logistic-regression coefficient vector), then ranking by ADC approximates ranking by the optimal spectral classifier; we test this alignment directly (Results).
We quantify this alignment using the Pearson correlation between the sensitivity vector $\partial \text{ADC}/\partial \Rvec$ and the logistic-regression coefficient vector $\mathbf{w}$ (fit on standardized fractions).
