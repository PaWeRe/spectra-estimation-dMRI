\section{Theory}

\subsection{Signal Model}

In multi-b diffusion MRI, the signal decay at each voxel or region of interest is modeled as a superposition of exponential decays arising from distinct diffusion compartments.
Two fundamentally different estimation strategies exist: (1)~treat both the diffusivity values $D_j$ and their corresponding fractions as free parameters to be estimated jointly from the data, or (2)~fix the diffusivity values on a predefined grid and estimate only the fractions.
The first approach is highly nonlinear and suffers from severe non-uniqueness, while the second reduces the problem to linear estimation at the cost of discretization.
We adopt the second approach: normalizing by the $b = 0$ signal, the discrete spectral model is:
%
(@comment stephan: S not s (throughout the document))
\begin{equation}
  \frac{S(b_i)}{S_0} = \sum_{j=1}^{K} R_j \exp(-b_i D_j) + \varepsilon_i,
  \quad i = 1, \ldots, M,
  \label{eq:signal_model}
\end{equation}
%
where $R_j \geq 0$ are the spectral fractions (volume fractions of each diffusion compartment), $D_j$ are the diffusivity bin centers, and $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ is additive Gaussian noise.
In matrix notation, $\svec / S_0 = \Umat \Rvec + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, where the design matrix $\Umat \in \mathbb{R}^{M \times K}$ has entries $U_{ij} = \exp(-b_i D_j)$.

For this study, $M = 15$ b-values spanning $0$--$3500\;\text{s/mm}^2$ in steps of $250\;\text{s/mm}^2$ and $K = 8$ diffusivity bins at $D \in \{0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0, 3.0, 20.0\}\;\umsmm$.
The design matrix $\Umat$ has condition number $\kappa(\Umat) \approx 2.8 \times 10^5$, reflecting the inherent ill-posedness of the inverse problem.

\subsection{Identifiability and Discretization}

The choice of diffusivity bins $D_j$ determines which spectral components can be resolved from the data.
The Fisher information matrix for the spectral fractions $\Rvec$ (assuming known noise $\sigma$) is:
%
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \Umat^T \Umat,
  \quad F_{jk} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \exp\!\big(-b_i(D_j + D_k)\big).
  \label{eq:fisher}
\end{equation}
%
The Cram\'er-Rao lower bound (CRLB) provides the minimum achievable variance for any unbiased estimator: $\text{Var}(\hat{R}_j) \geq [\mathbf{F}^{-1}]_{jj}$.
For our measurement configuration, the Fisher matrix has condition number $\kappa(\mathbf{F}) \approx 7.7 \times 10^{10}$, indicating that the unconstrained estimation problem is severely ill-conditioned.

The off-diagonal structure of $\mathbf{F}$ reveals why: adjacent diffusivity components produce nearly identical signal contributions.
The Fisher correlation matrix $C_{jk} = F_{jk} / \sqrt{F_{jj} F_{kk}}$ shows that components at $D = 0.50$, $0.75$, and $1.00\;\umsmm$ are correlated above $r = 0.99$ (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a), making them fundamentally indistinguishable at clinical noise levels.
In contrast, $D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ (restricted diffusion) contributes detectable signal across all 15 b-values, while fast-diffusing components ($D \geq 3.0$) decay below the noise floor within the first few b-value steps (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}c).

This resolving limitation can be quantified analytically.
For a simplified two-component model with adjacent diffusivities $D$ and $D + \Delta D$, the Fisher information for the fraction parameter approximately scales (@comment stephan: "approximately scales". This is for a two-compartment solution. I don't know how this holds for multi-compartment.) as
%
\begin{equation}
  I \propto \frac{(\Delta D)^2}{D^3}
  \label{eq:fisher_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
in the regime $\Delta D \ll D$ and $b_{\max} D \gg 1$ (see Supporting Information for derivation).
Requiring constant estimation precision across the spectrum therefore implies a minimum resolvable bin spacing that grows as
%
\begin{equation}
  \Delta D \propto D^{3/2}.
  \label{eq:spacing_scaling}
\end{equation}
%
Our discretization grid approximately follows this scaling: the spacing increases from $\Delta D = 0.25\;\umsmm$ at the slow-diffusion end to $\Delta D = 1.0\;\umsmm$ at $D = 2.0$, placing denser bins where all or most b-values contribute informative signal.
Numerical inversion of the full $8 \times 8$ Fisher matrix confirms that intermediate components ($D = 0.50$--$1.50\;\umsmm$) remain poorly conditioned regardless of the specific bin placement: redistributing bins according to the $D^{3/2}$ rule trades reduced resolution at high diffusivities for improved resolution at the slow-diffusion end, without reducing overall ill-conditioning.
The ill-posedness is intrinsic to the Laplace inversion and cannot be eliminated by optimizing the discretization alone.

The unconstrained CRLB vastly overestimates the actual estimation error: for intermediate components, the CRLB predicts standard deviations exceeding the physical range of the fractions (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}b).

The non-negativity constraint and Bayesian priors reduce the effective estimation error by two to three orders of magnitude, making the problem tractable.
This improvement is greatest for the most ill-conditioned components and demonstrates the essential role of prior information in spectral decomposition.

\subsection{MAP Estimation}

The maximum \emph{a~posteriori} estimate under a Gaussian (ridge) prior $R_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda^{-1})$ is obtained in closed form:
%
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\Rvec}_{\text{MAP}} = \max\!\Big(
    (\Umat^T \Umat + \lambda \mathbf{I})^{-1} \Umat^T (\svec / S_0),\;
    \mathbf{0}
  \Big),
  \label{eq:map}
\end{equation}
%
where $\lambda > 0$ controls the regularization strength and the $\max(\cdot, \mathbf{0})$ operator enforces non-negativity by projection.
This is equivalent to ridge regression with a non-negativity constraint (Ridge NNLS).
The MAP estimate is a point estimate and does not quantify how reliably each component $R_j$ is determined by the data.

\subsection{Bayesian Formulation}
(@comment stephan: better motivaiton why fully bayesian is cool and beneficial here (meainng etsimating SNR as well as R))

The MAP estimate provides a single point in spectrum space but offers no information about the reliability of each component.
Given the severe ill-conditioning documented above, this is a critical limitation: a point estimate at an intermediate component ($D = 0.75\;\umsmm$) may appear precise while in fact being largely determined by the regularization rather than the data.
A fully Bayesian approach addresses this by characterizing the entire posterior distribution, making the ill-posedness explicit rather than hiding it behind a single number.

We specify a hierarchical model with explicit priors on both the spectrum and the noise level:
%
\begin{align}
  R_j &\sim \text{HalfNormal}\!\left(\sigma_R = 1/\sqrt{\lambda}\right),
  \quad j = 1, \ldots, K \label{eq:prior_R} \\
  \sigma &\sim \text{HalfCauchy}(\beta = 0.01) \label{eq:prior_sigma} \\
  \svec / S_0 \mid \Rvec, \sigma &\sim \mathcal{N}(\Umat \Rvec,\; \sigma^2 \mathbf{I}).
  \label{eq:likelihood}
\end{align}
%
The HalfNormal prior on $\Rvec$ naturally enforces non-negativity while providing smooth gradients for Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC), and its variance $1/\lambda$ corresponds to the ridge penalty strength, establishing a direct link between the MAP and Bayesian formulations.
The HalfCauchy prior on the noise level $\sigma$ is a robust weakly informative choice \citep{gelman2006prior} that allows the noise to be inferred jointly with the spectrum, rather than fixed \emph{a priori}.

The posterior $p(\Rvec, \sigma \mid \svec, \Umat)$ is sampled using the No-U-Turn Sampler (NUTS) \citep{hoffman2014nuts}.
Standard Gibbs sampling is ineffective for this model because the HalfNormal priors are not conjugate to the Gaussian likelihood and, more fundamentally, the strong correlations between adjacent spectral components (Figure~\ref{fig:fisher}a) create a posterior geometry where coordinate-wise updates mix extremely slowly.
NUTS, as an adaptive variant of HMC \citep{neal2011mcmc}, exploits gradient information to propose joint moves across all components simultaneously, and automatically adapts the trajectory length to the local curvature of the posterior, making it well suited to the correlated, constrained geometry of this problem.

Posterior summaries (mean, standard deviation) for each $R_j$ provide both a point estimate and a measure of identifiability: components with posterior coefficient of variation (CV = posterior std / posterior mean) much less than one are well constrained, while those with CV near or above one are effectively undetermined by the data.

\subsection{ADC as a Spectral Functional}

The apparent diffusion coefficient can be viewed as a functional of the spectral fractions.
Given a spectrum $\Rvec$, the synthesized signal at each b-value is $\hat{S}(b) = \sum_j R_j \exp(-b D_j)$.

\subsection{ADC Sensitivity Analysis}
(@comment stpehan: You don't need this title, but you can certainly start a new paragraph.)
To understand which spectral components ADC is most sensitive to, we define the sensitivity vector:
%
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \,\text{ADC}}{\partial R_j}\bigg|_{\Rvec = \bar{\Rvec}}
  \approx \frac{\text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec} + \epsilon \, \mathbf{e}_j) - \text{ADC}(\bar{\Rvec})}{\epsilon},
  \label{eq:sensitivity}
\end{equation}
%
evaluated at a reference spectrum $\bar{\Rvec}$ (e.g., the average tumor or normal spectrum).
This sensitivity vector quantifies how a unit increase in each spectral fraction perturbs the ADC value.
If the sensitivity vector aligns with the optimal classification direction (the logistic regression coefficient vector), then ADC implicitly performs near-optimal spectral discrimination---it weights each compartment in proportion to its discriminative value.
We quantify this alignment using the Pearson correlation between the sensitivity vector $\partial \text{ADC}/\partial \Rvec$ and the standardized logistic regression coefficients $\mathbf{w}$.
